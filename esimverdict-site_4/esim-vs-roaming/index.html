Travel eSIM vs Roaming: Why Use an eSIM Abroad?

eSIM or roaming for your next trip? We compare 2026 daily roaming charges from EE, Vodafone, O2, Three, AT&T, Verizon and T-Mobile against travel eSIM prices.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 18 July 2026 15 min read

Some links on eSIMVerdict are affiliate links. If you buy through them we may earn a commission at no extra cost to you. Commercial relationships never affect rankings or conclusions; our assessments are based on independent research.

Should you switch on roaming or install a travel eSIM for your next trip? The honest answer is that it depends on your carrier, your destination and how long you are away. For a UK customer on O2 heading to Spain, roaming is free up to a generous fair-use cap and an eSIM would be a waste of money. For an American on AT&T spending ten days in Italy, roaming costs $120 while a comparable unlimited eSIM costs about $35.

The gap has widened in 2026. Three is raising its Go Roam charges to £2.75 a day in Europe and £8 a day elsewhere for newer customers, AT&T and Verizon both now charge $12 a day for their travel passes, and travel eSIM prices have kept falling, with US plans starting at $3.50 and unlimited Europe plans at around $3.50 a day over a ten-day trip.

This guide sets out the current roaming charges from the four big UK networks and the three big US carriers, compares them against verified eSIM prices from Airalo, Holafly, Nomad, MobiMatter and Holiday.com, and works through two ten-day trip examples. It also explains the dual-SIM setup that lets you keep your own number active for texts and banking codes while an eSIM handles the data.

At a glance

  • Roaming wins when your plan already includes it: O2 in Europe (free up to 25GB), EE's newer Essentials Plus, All Rounder and Full Works plans in the EU, Vodafone Xtra plans with inclusive roaming in 84 destinations, and T-Mobile's Experience plans with 5GB to 15GB of high-speed data in 215+ countries.
  • Roaming also wins for one-day or two-day trips, and when you need your own number for calls and SMS.
  • eSIMs win on cost almost everywhere roaming is charged daily: $12 a day on AT&T or Verizon versus roughly $3.50 a day for unlimited eSIM data in Europe or the US.
  • eSIMs win on long trips and multi-country itineraries, since regional plans cover 30-42 European countries on one eSIM with no daily meter running.
  • The best setup for most travellers is both at once: home SIM kept active for texts and one-time passcodes with data roaming off, travel eSIM set as the data line.
  • Most travel eSIMs are data-only: no conventional calls or SMS, so WhatsApp and similar apps carry your conversations.

When roaming is genuinely the better choice

Balance matters here, because roaming is not always the rip-off it is painted as.

Your plan already includes it. O2 in Europe, EE's post-August 2024 plans in the EU, Vodafone Xtra, Three's Value and Complete plans, and T-Mobile's Experience plans all make an eSIM redundant within their coverage and caps.

Short trips. A weekend in Paris on EE costs £4.94 in daily charges. Most eSIM plans are priced for five days or more, so a two-day plan rarely undercuts that by enough to justify the setup.

You need your own number. Roaming keeps your normal number live for calls and SMS. That matters for banking one-time passcodes, deliveries, work calls and anyone who might ring you. Travel eSIMs from all five providers above are data-only, so calls and texts to your real number do not reach the eSIM. The dual-SIM setup below solves most of this, but people who take a lot of voice calls abroad are better served by a day pass.

Older handsets. Phones without eSIM support, or region-locked models, leave roaming as the only option short of buying a physical local SIM.

When an eSIM clearly wins

Daily-charge destinations. Anywhere your carrier meters by the day at $12, £7 or £8, an eSIM typically cuts the cost by 60-80 per cent over a week or more.

Long trips. Daily charges compound; eSIM plans are priced per gigabyte or per fixed period. Thirty days in the US on Verizon is $360; Airalo's 30-day unlimited US plan is $69.

Multiple countries. A regional eSIM covers 30-42 European countries on one plan, and marketplace or multi-region providers stretch further. Our multi-destination guide compares the options.

Data caps. Three's 12GB and O2's and Vodafone's 25GB roaming caps are hard limits for heavy users; a 50GB Nomad Europe plan costs $35.

Budget certainty. You pay up front, and there is no risk of a forgotten background sync triggering another day's charge on the flight home. If price is the overriding factor, see our best budget eSIM ranking.

The dual-SIM setup: keep your number, use eSIM data

Every eSIM-capable phone can run two lines at once, and this is the configuration our research suggests most travellers should use.

  1. Install and activate the travel eSIM before departure (providers recommend installing on Wi-Fi at home).
  2. In settings, set the travel eSIM as your mobile data line.
  3. Keep your home SIM switched on, but turn data roaming off for that line only.
  4. Leave your home number as the default for calls and SMS.

The result: all your data flows through the cheap eSIM, while your home SIM stays registered on a local network to receive texts. Receiving an SMS abroad is free on the UK networks and does not trigger a daily roaming charge, so banking one-time passcodes arrive at no cost. Be aware that answering a voice call or sending a text on your home SIM can trigger that day's roaming charge or per-minute fees, so let non-urgent calls go to voicemail and reply on WhatsApp. Some banks deliver codes only by SMS, which is exactly why keeping the home SIM active matters; app-based authenticators avoid the issue entirely.

Android users should note that some eSIMs, including Holiday.com's on certain handsets, need APN settings entered manually; iPhones generally configure themselves.

Advantages and disadvantages

Roaming: advantages. Zero setup; your own number for calls, SMS and passcodes; one bill; day passes include voice; genuinely free for O2 (Europe), inclusive-plan EE and Vodafone customers, and T-Mobile Experience customers within their allowances.

Roaming: disadvantages. Daily charges of $12, £7-£8 outside inclusive zones; fair-use caps as low as 12GB; speed caps (O2 Travel's 2Mbps, Verizon's post-5GB 3G speeds, T-Mobile's 256Kbps baseline); prices have risen in 2025-2026 and bill shock remains possible on pay-per-use rates.

eSIMs: advantages. Data from $3.49; fixed up-front cost; regional multi-country coverage; instant delivery and activation; no physical SIM to lose; easy top-ups with most providers.

eSIMs: disadvantages. Data-only on the major travel brands; "unlimited" almost always carries a daily high-speed cap; quality depends on which local network the provider partners with; support is chat and email rather than a phone line; a second thing to set up and manage.

Who should choose what

  • O2 customers going to Europe: use your roaming. Do not buy anything.
  • EE, Vodafone or Three customers on inclusive-roaming plans: check your plan documents first; many of you also need nothing, within the fair-use caps.
  • UK travellers to the USA or Asia on standard plans: an eSIM, or EE's £25 Roam Abroad Pass if you want your number fully live.
  • AT&T and Verizon customers on any trip over three days: an eSIM saves serious money; keep TravelPass or Day Pass for the odd day you must make lengthy calls.
  • T-Mobile Experience customers: your included 5GB or 15GB covers light and moderate use; add an eSIM only if you stream heavily or work remotely.
  • Multi-country itineraries and trips over two weeks: regional or global eSIMs, almost without exception.
  • Travellers who live on voice calls to their own number: roaming day passes, despite the cost.

The verdict

Check what your plan already includes before you spend a penny; in 2026 that single step settles the question for millions of O2, inclusive-plan and T-Mobile customers. Where roaming is metered by the day, the numbers are no longer close: $120 against about $35 for ten days in Europe on a US carrier, and £70-£80 against about £25 for ten days in the US on a UK daily-charge plan. For everyone in that position, a travel eSIM for data plus your home SIM kept alive for texts is the cheapest reliable setup, and it is the one we would use ourselves.

Frequently asked questions

Is an eSIM always cheaper than roaming?

No. O2 includes EU roaming free up to 25GB, several EE, Vodafone and Three plans include roaming, and T-Mobile bundles 5GB-15GB of international data. eSIMs are cheaper mainly where carriers charge daily fees: $12 a day on AT&T and Verizon, or £7-£8 a day for UK networks outside Europe.

Can I keep my WhatsApp number with a travel eSIM?

Yes. WhatsApp is tied to your registered phone number and account, not to the SIM carrying your data. Keep your home SIM installed, use the eSIM for data, and WhatsApp continues on your normal number.

Will I still get banking SMS codes if I use a travel eSIM?

Yes, provided you leave your home SIM active in dual-SIM mode. Receiving texts abroad is free on the UK networks and does not trigger daily roaming charges. Only turn off data roaming on the home line, not the line itself.

Do travel eSIMs include calls and texts?

The major travel eSIM brands, including Airalo, Holafly, Nomad and Holiday.com, sell data-only plans in most destinations. Voice and SMS run over apps such as WhatsApp or FaceTime instead. If you need conventional calls on your own number, a carrier day pass or monthly pass is the better tool.

What happened to Three's roaming charges after the Vodafone merger?

Vodafone and Three completed their merger on 31 May 2025, forming VodafoneThree, though both brands still operate separately. From 1 April 2026 Three's Go Roam charges rose to £2.75 a day in Europe and £8 a day worldwide for customers who joined or upgraded from January 2026; those who joined between October 2021 and December 2025 currently keep the old £2 and £7 rates.
