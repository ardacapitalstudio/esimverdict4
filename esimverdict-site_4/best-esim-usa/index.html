Best eSIM for the United States in 2026: Five Providers Ranked and Compared

We compared USA eSIMs from Airalo, Nomad, Holiday.com, Holafly and MobiMatter on price, networks and hotspot rules. See which is best for your 2026 trip.

The best eSIM for the United States in 2026 depends less on headline price and more on which American network you end up connected to. The USA has three national carriers - T-Mobile, AT&T and Verizon - and they behave very differently once you leave the cities. A travel eSIM that rides on T-Mobile will be quick in New York and San Francisco but can fade on rural highways, while AT&T and Verizon hold their signal far better across national parks and the long empty stretches between them.

Our research ranks Airalo first overall for the USA, thanks to the cheapest entry price of the group ($4.00 for 1GB), a huge range of plans including options with a +1 US phone number, and top-ups on one eSIM. Nomad is the pick for road trips because its USA plans can connect to AT&T and Verizon as well as T-Mobile. Holiday.com is the value option with the most usable "unlimited" policy, Holafly suits heavy data users who want one daily price, and MobiMatter is the bargain hunter's marketplace with the lowest cost per gigabyte we found.

Below we explain the methodology, compare prices per gigabyte and per unlimited day, and set out who each provider actually suits.

Why the network matters more in America than almost anywhere else

Distances in the USA are enormous, and unlike much of Europe you should not assume free wifi will bail you out. Cafés and hotels often have it, but motels on interstate routes, national parks and rural fuel stops frequently do not, and public networks are a poor place to do banking. Your eSIM is likely to be your only reliable connection for navigation across a 500-mile driving day.

The practical picture, based on carrier coverage data summarised by eSIM-Now's 2026 US coverage guide: all three networks claim roughly 99% population coverage on 4G LTE, but population coverage is not land coverage. T-Mobile has the widest 5G footprint and the fastest median 5G speeds, which makes T-Mobile-based eSIMs excellent in cities. Verizon has the strongest rural footprint and is the safer bet around Yellowstone, Zion, the Grand Canyon and the Mountain West. AT&T sits in between, with broad coverage across both cities and most rural corridors. Along major interstates you will have near-continuous 4G/5G with any of them; the gaps appear on secondary highways, in deserts and canyons, and across the Great Plains.

One more American quirk: many US services - banks, ticketing platforms, even some restaurant waitlists - verify users by SMS to a US number. Almost every travel eSIM is data-only, so those texts never arrive. If that matters to you, Airalo is currently the only provider in this group selling USA plans with a genuine +1 number for calls and texts.

Airalo - best overall for the USA

Airalo's United States page lists plans from $4.00, and the independent plan tracker esimdb (updated 12 July 2026) counts 65 USA options: 1GB at $4.00, 3GB from $8.50, 5GB from $12.50, 10GB from $21.50, 20GB from $36.00 and 50GB at $42.00, plus unlimited plans from $11.50 for 3 days up to $69.00 for 30 days. That 30-day unlimited works out at $2.30 per day, the cheapest per-day unlimited rate of the big brands here. The important caveat: Airalo's "unlimited" USA plans give 3GB per day at full speed, after which speeds drop to around 1 Mbps until the next day. That is enough for maps and messaging, not for video.

Airalo's USA eSIMs connect primarily to T-Mobile, with the site listing one additional partner network. That means excellent city performance and wide 5G, but it is the weakest of the three carriers in remote rural areas. Its Change+ and similar packages add a +1 US number with calls and SMS, which solves the verification problem no data-only rival can. Tethering is allowed - Airalo's help centre states you can use a personal hotspot wherever your device and the network support it. The apps are the best regarded in the category (4.7 on iOS, 4.6 on Android), and top-ups mean one eSIM lasts across repeat trips. Support is by email and in-app chat rather than a genuine 24/7 phone-style service. For a fuller picture, see our Airalo review.

Nomad - best for road trips and rural coverage

Nomad's USA page lists 1GB for $5.00 (7 days), 3GB for $10.00, 5GB for $16.00, 10GB for $25.00, 20GB for $34.00 and 50GB for $45.00 (all 30 days), plus long-stay options up to 100GB over a year for $120.00. Unlimited runs from $15.00 for 3 days to $65.00 for 20 days, with speeds throttled to 512 kbps after the daily high-speed allowance - a harsher step-down than Airalo's 1 Mbps or Holiday.com's 10 Mbps.

Its trump card is networks. Nomad states its USA service uses AT&T, Verizon and T-Mobile; a June 2026 review by Twise found its eSIM switching automatically between AT&T and Verizon for the strongest signal. For a coast-to-coast drive or a national parks itinerary, that combination is worth more than any price difference on this list. Hotspot is supported on all plans, top-up add-ons land on the same eSIM, and the 50GB plan's $0.90/GB is the best big-data rate outside the MobiMatter marketplace. The trade-offs: data-only on most plans, and eSIMs must be activated within 60 days of purchase.

Holiday.com - best value unlimited policy

Holiday.com, built by the ExpressVPN team as a standalone eSIM service (no VPN is included), covers 150+ destinations with USA plans from $3.50. Independent reviews list representative pricing of 1GB for $3.50, 5GB for $12.50 and 10GB for $20.00. What stands out is the unlimited policy, which the company states plainly: 3GB per day at high speed, then unlimited data at 10 Mbps. A 10 Mbps fallback still streams video and runs video calls, which makes this the most honest and most usable "unlimited" in this comparison.

You get a year from purchase to activate, one eSIM that re-tops for every trip, tethering support, and 24/7 live chat plus email. The weaknesses reflect its youth: the apps are newer than rivals', Android setup may need a manual APN entry, and Holiday.com does not name its US network partners, which costs it coverage points in our scoring. Customer reports from other destinations also note traffic can route through non-local IP addresses, which occasionally confuses location-based apps.

Holafly - best for heavy data users

Holafly sells only unlimited plans. Its USA eSIM runs on AT&T and T-Mobile with 5G where available, priced at $11.90 for 3 days, $27.50 for 7, $50.50 for 15 and $73.90 for 30 - between $2.46 and $3.97 per day depending on length. Unlike Airalo and Nomad, Holafly does not advertise a fixed daily high-speed cap for the USA, though its wider fair-use terms allow carriers to slow very heavy users, typically after around 3-5GB in a day, with speeds recovering the next day.

The big restriction is tethering: Holafly caps hotspot sharing at 1GB per day on its USA plans, so it is a poor choice if a laptop is your main device. There are no fixed-data options and no top-ups in the traditional sense (extensions run through the customer panel), so light users end up overpaying - a 30-day trip costs $73.90 whether you use 2GB or 60GB. In its favour: 24/7 chat support is genuinely round the clock, the AT&T/T-Mobile pairing covers cities and most corridors well, and a refund window of up to 6 months applies to eligible unused plans. We compare it with our top pick in Holafly vs Airalo comparison.

MobiMatter - cheapest per gigabyte, with caveats

MobiMatter is a marketplace of 30+ eSIM sellers rather than a single provider, and for the USA that produces the lowest prices in this article: NextLink-branded plans at $9.99 for 10GB, $12.99 for 15GB and $16.99 for 20GB (roughly $0.85-$1.00 per GB), unlimited from $1.80 per day, and headline offers from $0.70/GB. MobiMatter states its US plans run on the major carriers with 4G/5G nationwide, hotspot supported, and some plans even include voice and SMS.

The caveats are structural. Which network, which speed cap and which refund policy you get depends on the individual seller, so you must read each listing carefully - transparency and consistency are the weakest here. Support is likewise variable: MobiMatter handles the storefront, but plan problems often route back to the underlying provider. Confident, price-driven buyers who check the fine print can do very well; first-time eSIM users should probably pay a little more elsewhere. Our Holafly vs MobiMatter comparison comparison looks at the two extremes of this trade-off, and budget eSIM guide covers cheap options beyond the USA.

Advantages and disadvantages

Airalo - Pros: cheapest entry price, 65 plan choices, top-ups, US number option, best apps. Cons: T-Mobile-first coverage weakens in remote areas; unlimited throttles to 1 Mbps after 3GB/day.

Nomad - Pros: AT&T + Verizon + T-Mobile access, strong big-data value, hotspot on all plans. Cons: 512 kbps post-cap speed is barely usable; data-only on most plans; 60-day activation window.

Holiday.com - Pros: clearest unlimited policy (3GB/day then 10 Mbps), low prices, one reusable eSIM, 24/7 chat. Cons: unnamed US network partners; newer apps; possible manual APN setup on Android.

Holafly - Pros: no data rationing, AT&T/T-Mobile dual network, 24/7 support. Cons: expensive for light users, 1GB/day hotspot cap, no fixed-data plans, fair-use slowdowns possible.

MobiMatter - Pros: lowest per-GB prices, occasional voice/SMS plans, huge choice. Cons: quality, terms and support vary by seller; more homework required.

Who should choose what

  • City break (New York, Miami, LA): Airalo. T-Mobile is at its best in urban America, and $4.00 for 1GB or $12.50-$13.50 for 5GB covers a week of maps and social media.
  • Road trip or national parks: Nomad. Access to Verizon and AT&T matters more than price once you are two hours from an interstate.
  • Remote worker on a laptop: Holiday.com. The 10 Mbps unthrottled fallback plus normal tethering beats Holafly's 1GB/day hotspot cap.
  • Heavy streamer who hates counting gigabytes: Holafly, or Airalo's unlimited if you can live within 3GB/day at full speed.
  • Budget maximiser: MobiMatter's NextLink deals at around $1.00/GB, if you are comfortable reading seller terms.
  • Need a US number for SMS verification: Airalo's Change+ plans are the only real answer in this group.
  • Visiting several countries after the USA: see multi-destination eSIM guide, and for whether an eSIM beats your carrier's roaming pass at all, eSIM vs roaming guide.

The verdict

Airalo wins the USA in 2026, scoring 64/80. It is the cheapest way in at $4.00, the only provider here with a US number option, and its $69.00 30-day unlimited is the best per-day rate among the major brands - provided you accept the 3GB/day full-speed limit. Nomad finishes one point behind at 63/80 and is our outright recommendation for road trips, because AT&T and Verizon access solves the one problem Airalo cannot. Holiday.com (59/80) is the smart value pick with the most usable unlimited policy, Holafly (53/80) remains the simplest choice for genuinely heavy phone-based use, and MobiMatter (52/80) is where the cheapest gigabytes live for buyers who do their homework. Frequently asked questions

What is the best eSIM for the USA in 2026?

Our research ranks Airalo best overall for the USA, with plans from $4.00 for 1GB, unlimited from $2.30 per day over 30 days, top-ups and an optional +1 US number. Nomad is the better choice for rural travel because it can use AT&T and Verizon as well as T-Mobile.

Which US network will my travel eSIM use?

It depends on the provider: Airalo's USA plans are T-Mobile-first, Holafly uses AT&T and T-Mobile, Nomad lists AT&T, Verizon and T-Mobile, and MobiMatter varies by seller. Holiday.com does not name its partners. T-Mobile is strongest in cities; Verizon and AT&T hold up better in rural areas and national parks.

Are unlimited USA eSIMs really unlimited?

Rarely at full speed. Airalo gives 3GB/day fast then 1 Mbps, Nomad throttles to 512 kbps after its daily cap, and Holiday.com gives 3GB/day fast then unlimited data at 10 Mbps. Holafly advertises no fixed cap for the USA, but its fair-use terms allow slowdowns after roughly 3-5GB in a day on some carriers.

Can I receive SMS verification codes with a USA travel eSIM?

Not with most of them, because they are data-only and have no phone number. Airalo's Change+ USA plans include a +1 US number with calls and texts, which is currently the most practical option in this group for banking and booking codes.

How much data do I need for a two-week US trip?

Around 5-10GB covers maps, messaging and social media for most visitors; do not count on free wifi outside big cities and chain hotels. At current prices that is $12.50-$22.50 with Airalo, $16.00-$25.00 with Nomad, or $9.99-$12.99 via MobiMatter. Streamers and remote workers should pick an unlimited plan instead.
