Best eSIM for Low-Latency Video Calls: Zoom, Teams and Meet Abroad in 2026

Which eSIM handles Zoom, Teams and Meet best abroad? We compare routing, throttling floors and upload speeds to rank the best travel eSIMs for video calls.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 14 min read

The best eSIM for video calls is rarely the one with the biggest data bundle. Zoom, Teams and Meet care about three things a headline price never shows: how far your traffic travels before it reaches the open internet, what speed remains once a fair-use cap bites, and whether upload can carry your camera feed. A plan that streams Netflix flawlessly can still make you sound like a robot in Monday's stand-up: streaming tolerates seconds of buffering, a conversation does not.

Our research ranks Holiday.com first for remote workers living on video calls, because its unlimited plans fall back to 10 Mbps rather than the 1 Mbps floor most rivals impose, and 10 Mbps still carries a 720p group call. Ubigi is the pick for network quality, running on NTT-owned infrastructure with unlimited options and wide 5G support. Saily is the polished budget route, Holafly suits phone-only callers who want one daily price, and MobiMatter's marketplace is where you find local-operator plans whose traffic breaks out in the country you are actually standing in - the single biggest latency win available. Below we set out the bandwidth each platform officially demands, explain why travel eSIM routing quietly ruins calls, and rank five providers.

What video calls actually need

The platforms publish exact numbers, more modest than most people assume - until the camera comes on in a group call.

Bandwidth is only half the story. ITU-T Recommendation G.114, the standard telephone engineers plan around, treats one-way delay of up to 150 ms as acceptable for most applications, 150-400 ms as tolerable with degraded interactivity, and anything above 400 ms as unacceptable. Microsoft's targets for Teams media are stricter still: round-trip time under 60 ms to its network edge is optimal and over 500 ms is classed as poor, average packet loss should sit under 0.5% (over 10% is poor), and jitter - the variation in packet arrival times - is ideally under 3 ms, with over 30 ms classed as poor. Cross those thresholds and you get the familiar symptoms: talking over each other, frozen tiles, and audio that arrives in bursts.

Why travel eSIM routing hurts calls

Here is the part almost no provider advertises. Most travel eSIMs are roaming products: your data does not exit to the internet in the country you are visiting. Instead it is tunnelled back to the sponsor network's home gateway - the "breakout" point - which may be in Europe, Singapore or Hong Kong regardless of where you are. Our guide to eSIM traffic routing explains the mechanics and why your IP address often shows another country.

For browsing, this adds a delay you barely notice. For a live call it is decisive: every packet pays the detour twice, for the whole meeting.

The worst case is the double hop. Say you are working from Bali and your company runs on a US-hosted Teams tenant. With an eSIM that breaks out in Frankfurt, your audio travels Bali to Frankfurt, then Frankfurt to the US meeting server, and the reply makes the same journey in reverse. Fibre paths of that length add hundreds of milliseconds of round-trip time - beyond the ITU's 150 ms comfort zone on geometry alone - while the same plan used from Lisbon, two short hops from Frankfurt, works beautifully. This is why customer reports on identical plans range from "flawless" to "unusable": breakout distance, not advertised speed, is doing the damage.

The practical consequences: prefer providers that break out on your working continent or offer a choice of gateway (Ubigi lets users select an IP location), consider a genuinely local plan via a marketplace such as MobiMatter, and treat speed tests with suspicion - they measure throughput to a nearby server, not the round trip your meeting audio makes.

Fair-use throttling: when unlimited means audio-only

"Unlimited" plans nearly always have a daily high-speed allowance, and what happens afterwards decides whether your afternoon meetings survive. Airalo's unlimited plans give 3GB per day at full speed and then drop to around 1 Mbps; Nomad's give 2GB per day before a similar step-down (512 kbps on some plans). Check those floors against the table above: 1 Mbps fails Zoom's 2.6/1.8 Mbps requirement for a 720p group call, though it still carries audio plus screen share. Once the cap bites you are running an audio-only service, whatever "unlimited" suggested. Our Airalo review covers the wider service in detail.

Two policies stand out. Holiday.com states its unlimited plans fall back to 10 Mbps after 3GB/day at high speed - a floor that clears every standard-quality requirement in the table. Holafly publishes no fixed daily cap at all, though its fair-use terms allow carrier-level slowdowns for very heavy users, typically after roughly 3-5GB in a day on some networks - harder to plan around than a stated number.

Upload: the neglected half of the connection

Mobile networks are asymmetric: download is the number networks advertise, upload is the number video calls die on. With your camera on, you are continuously transmitting 600 kbps to 3.8 Mbps depending on quality tier, and upload is the first thing to collapse on a congested cell at 9 am in a co-working district. When a call degrades, it is usually your outbound video that freezes first - everyone else looks fine to you, while you have frozen for them.

5G helps here more than its headline download figures suggest, because uplink capacity and latency both improve. Ubigi advertises 4G/5G access across its footprint and Holafly connects to 5G where its partners support it; whether you actually get 5G depends on the local network, not the eSIM brand. If your provider offers a choice of partner networks, test upload on each: a "slower" network with double the uplink is the better meeting connection.

How much data does an hour of calls use?

An hour of calling cannot exceed roughly 0.45GB per Mbps of combined up and down bandwidth, so the platforms' own maximum rates set safe budgets:

  • Audio-only (any platform): roughly 30-90MB per hour
  • Zoom group call, standard quality (1.0/0.6 Mbps): up to ~0.7GB per hour
  • Zoom group call at 720p (2.6/1.8 Mbps): up to ~2GB per hour
  • 1080p on any platform (roughly 4/3 Mbps): up to ~3GB per hour

A camera-on, four-meeting day can therefore burn 3-6GB - exactly why the 3GB/day high-speed allowances on unlimited plans matter, and why fixed 5GB bundles vanish faster than travellers expect. Our guide to how much eSIM data you need has fuller day-by-day budgets.

1. Holiday.com - best overall for video calls

Holiday.com, built by the ExpressVPN team as a standalone eSIM (no VPN is included), covers 150+ destinations with plans from $3.50. It ranks first here for one published sentence: unlimited plans deliver 3GB per day at high speed, then unlimited data at 10 Mbps. That floor clears Zoom's 720p group-call requirement with headroom, so a heavy meeting day degrades gracefully instead of collapsing to audio. Tethering is supported normally, one eSIM re-tops for every trip, and support runs 24/7 on live chat and email. The caveats: it is the newest brand in this ranking, Android setup can require a manual APN entry, it does not name network partners per country, and customer reports note traffic can surface from non-local IP addresses - a routing consideration if your meetings are latency-critical.

2. Ubigi - best network pedigree and 5G

Ubigi is a brand of Transatel | NTT, which means the connectivity is run by a global carrier rather than a reseller, and it shows in the details relevant to calls: 200+ destinations, 4G and 5G access, tethering support, and unusually for this market an IP location option that lets users choose how their traffic surfaces. Unlimited plans are available in supported destinations on 8-day ($49), 15-day ($77) and 30-day ($119) terms, with a monthly subscription at $140. It is not the cheap option - one-off gigabytes cost more than Airalo or Saily - and support is not Holafly's round-the-clock chat. But for a consultant who bills by the hour, infrastructure quality is the right thing to pay for.

3. Saily - best budget all-rounder

Saily comes from the team behind NordVPN and is the most polished of the cheaper options: 4.7-rated on the App Store (35,000+ reviews), Google Play (61,000+) and Trustpilot, with unlimited plans in many destinations alongside small fixed bundles. Two call-specific notes. Saily does not publish a single uniform fair-use policy, so check the stated cap on your destination's unlimited plan before trusting it with your meetings. And its security extras - virtual location and web protection - deliberately reroute traffic, the opposite of what a low-latency call wants; turn virtual location off during working hours. It is the sensible pick when budget matters; see our best budget eSIM guide for cheaper still.

4. Holafly - unlimited for phone-only callers

Holafly's pitch fits heavy callers on paper: every plan unlimited, 220+ destinations, from $6.90/day, monthly global plans around $64.90, and genuinely 24/7 chat support. Two things hold it back for remote workers. The hotspot cap of 1GB per day on single-trip plans is roughly thirty minutes of 720p group calling from a tethered laptop - if your meetings happen on a laptop, that is disqualifying. And the absence of a published fair-use threshold makes a meeting-heavy day hard to plan, since carrier-level slowdowns are reported at roughly 3-5GB on some networks. If you take every call on your phone and want zero data anxiety, it works well; the Trustpilot score of about 4.6 is the strongest of the big brands.

5. MobiMatter - the local-breakout hunting ground

MobiMatter is a marketplace of 30+ providers across 190+ destinations with plans from $3.49, and it earns its place for a specific manoeuvre: some listings are genuine local-operator plans whose traffic breaks out in-country, eliminating the roaming detour that adds hundreds of milliseconds to everyone else's calls. Some even include voice and SMS. The trade-offs are structural: network, speed policy and refund terms depend on the individual seller, and support often routes back to the underlying provider, so read each listing carefully. For a confident buyer settling into one country for a month, a local plan found here can outperform everything above it on latency; for a first-time eSIM user mid-trip, the homework is real.

Advantages and disadvantages of travel eSIMs for video calls

Advantages: independence from congested hotel and café wifi; instant failover when wifi dies mid-meeting; unlimited options that remove data anxiety on camera-heavy days; a tethered phone can serve a laptop anywhere with signal.

Disadvantages: roaming-style routing adds latency wifi does not; 1 Mbps fair-use floors quietly demote you to audio-only; upload on congested cells fails before download; hotspot caps (notably Holafly's 1GB/day) limit laptop use; data-only plans cannot take ordinary phone calls.

Who should choose what

  • Daily meetings from a laptop, mixed destinations: Holiday.com. The 10 Mbps floor plus normal tethering is the safest combination here.
  • Latency-critical calls: Ubigi, or a local-operator plan via MobiMatter if you are staying in one country.
  • Budget-conscious with a few calls a week: Saily, with virtual location off during calls.
  • Phone-only callers who hate counting gigabytes: Holafly, accepting the hotspot cap.
  • Meetings that must not drop: pair any pick with wifi failover; our eSIM reliability guide explains dual-path setups, and running two eSIMs at once covers backup lines.
  • On a company phone or laptop: check MDM restrictions first; see our guide to eSIMs on corporate devices.

Practical tactics that beat upgrading your plan

A few habits do more for call quality than any provider switch. Join important meetings with video off and screen share on: 50-250 kbps survives almost any throttle, and Zoom's audio needs only 60-80 kbps. Set meeting apps to standard definition rather than HD; on a phone screen the difference is invisible and the bandwidth saving is large. Schedule around congestion where you can - cell uplink is worst at co-working peak hours, so an 8 am call often outperforms an 11 am one on the same plan. Keep wifi and your eSIM both active so the laptop fails over rather than drops. And if you run a VPN for work, it adds its own hop on top of your eSIM's breakout - our best eSIM for VPN users guide covers picking exit servers that minimise the damage.

Frequently asked questions

How much data does an hour of Zoom use on an eSIM?

At Zoom's published rates, an audio-only hour uses roughly 30-90MB, a standard-quality group call up to about 0.7GB, and a 720p group call up to about 2GB. Adaptive codecs usually use less, but a camera-on meeting day can still reach 3-6GB.

Why is my video call laggy abroad even though my speed test is fast?

Speed tests measure throughput to a nearby server; calls depend on the round trip to the meeting server. Most travel eSIMs tunnel traffic to a home gateway on another continent before it reaches the internet, so packets detour thousands of kilometres each way. A local-operator plan, or a provider with nearby breakout, fixes what extra megabits cannot.

Are unlimited eSIMs good enough for video calls?

Only if the post-cap speed floor is. Airalo and Nomad throttle to around 1 Mbps or less after their daily allowances, below what a 720p group call needs. Holiday.com's 10 Mbps floor keeps video usable; Holafly publishes no fixed cap but allows carrier fair-use slowdowns.

Is 1 Mbps enough for Zoom or Teams?

For audio plus screen sharing, comfortably: Zoom audio needs 60-80 kbps and screen share 50-75 kbps. For video it is marginal at best, and well below group-call requirements. Treat a 1 Mbps throttle as audio-only.

Should I use hotel wifi or my eSIM for important calls?

Test both and keep both connected. Hotel wifi is often congested but has local breakout; your eSIM is usually less congested but routes further. Join from the better performer and leave the other active as failover so a dropout switches paths instead of ending the call.
