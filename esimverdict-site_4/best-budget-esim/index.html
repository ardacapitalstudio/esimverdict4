Best Budget-Friendly eSIMs in 2026: Cheap Plans Ranked by Real Value Per Gigabyte

We compared budget eSIMs from MobiMatter, Nomad, Saily, Airalo and more, ranking real cost per usable gigabyte and exposing the traps behind cheap headline prices.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 18 July 2026 13 min read

Some links on eSIMVerdict are affiliate links. If you buy through them we may earn a commission at no extra cost to you. Commercial relationships never affect rankings or conclusions; our assessments are based on independent research.

The best budget eSIM is rarely the one with the lowest "from" price on the homepage. A $3.49 banner can hide a plan that exists in one country, expires in seven days, and forfeits every unused megabyte the moment the clock runs out. What matters is the price per usable gigabyte: what you actually pay, divided by the data you can realistically consume within the validity window.

Our research on 18 July 2026 compared current prices across MobiMatter, Nomad, Airalo, Holiday.com and three smaller players (Saily, aloSIM and Yesim), using Thailand as a like-for-like test destination with Turkey as a cross-check. The gap is startling. On the same Thai networks, real cost ranges from about $0.20 per gigabyte on a MobiMatter marketplace deal to $5.00 per gigabyte on a small starter plan, a 25x difference.

This guide ranks the genuinely cheap options, shows the per-gigabyte maths, and walks through the six traps that make "budget" eSIMs quietly expensive.

At a glance

  • Best budget eSIM overall: Nomad - consistently low per-gigabyte prices with clearly documented terms, strongest in Asia-Pacific
  • Cheapest absolute prices: MobiMatter - marketplace deals from $3.99 headline, and as low as $0.20/GB in Thailand, if you accept stricter refund terms
  • Cheapest starter plans: Saily - 1GB in Thailand for $2.99, backed by Nord Security
  • Best budget top-up model: Holiday.com - plans from $3.50, one eSIM you re-top for every trip, 1-year activation window
  • Honourable mention: aloSIM - Thailand 1GB at $3.50 and 20GB at $19.50 from a small Canadian outfit
  • Losing its budget crown: Airalo - "from $4.00" survives on regional plans, but several country stores now sell only pricier unlimited packages

How we ranked these providers

Every eSIMVerdict ranking uses the same eight criteria: price, coverage, network quality, plan flexibility, transparency, ease of use, hotspot support and customer service. For this budget guide we weighted price at 25% and transparency at 20%, because a cheap plan with hidden conditions is not cheap. The remaining six criteria share the balance. Prices were verified on official provider sites on 18 July 2026; policy details come from provider terms, help centres and reputable independent reviews. We have not personally tested every service, and we say so where a claim rests on customer reports rather than our own verification.

1. Nomad - best budget eSIM overall

Nomad advertises plans from about $3.50 for 1GB, covers 200+ destinations, and is at its sharpest in Asia-Pacific. Our checks found Thailand at $5.00 for 1GB/7 days but a strong $6.00 for 3GB/30 days and $10.00 for 10GB/30 days on AIS/dtac, and Turkey at $4.00 for 1GB and $14.00 for 10GB on Turk Telekom. Regional Asia-Pacific pricing runs as low as roughly $2.17/GB on larger multi-country plans.

What earns Nomad the top slot is transparency at a low price. The throttle speed after unlimited-plan daily allowances is stated plainly (512kbps), hotspot use is confirmed on all plans, and the activation rule is explicit: activate within 60 days of purchase or the plan auto-activates and starts expiring on its own. That last clause is also your homework - buy close to departure. Top-up add-ons exist but availability varies by plan, and reinstallation policies differ between plans, so frequent travellers should check before relying on one eSIM long-term.

Who should avoid it: travellers who need calls and texts (Nomad is data-only), and anyone who buys eSIMs months in advance.

2. MobiMatter - cheapest per gigabyte, with strings attached

MobiMatter is a marketplace, not a network: 190+ destinations, 30+ providers including 3, Orange, Telenor, EE and Ubigi, with headline deals from $3.99 (Oceania 1GB) and a stack of aggressive offers. In Thailand the standout was the True "Happy Tourist" plan: 50GB for 10 days at $9.99, or $0.20 per gigabyte. A RoamVault 10GB/30-day plan cost $7.99, and a 3-network Singapore-Malaysia-Thailand 30GB plan cost $14.99. Turkey offered 12GB/30 days for $7.99. Nothing else in this guide gets close on raw price.

The strings are real, though. Refunds are only available if the eSIM is unused, unactivated and still inside its activation period, and MobiMatter's help centre notes that processing fees may apply. Because each plan comes from a different underlying operator, terms, APN settings, top-up availability and support quality vary from listing to listing, and public complaint boards carry reports of failed activations ending without refunds. Read the individual product description like a contract, because it is one.

Who should avoid it: first-time eSIM users and anyone who values a single accountable support team over the lowest price.

3. Saily - cheapest starter plans, clearest ownership

Saily is built by Nord Security, the company behind NordVPN, which answers the ownership question that hangs over many cheap eSIM brands. Independent testing by Cybernews puts its cheapest plan at $1.79 for 1GB/7 days, and our direct checks found Thailand at $2.99 for 1GB/7 days, $5.99 for 3GB/30 days, $10.99 for 10GB/30 days and $19.99 for 20GB/30 days, with an Asia-Oceania regional option from $4.99. Plans activate on arrival or 30 days after purchase, whichever comes first, and top-ups take a few taps in the app, with hotspot use unrestricted.

The catch list is short but worth knowing: the service is data-only unless you pay $0.99/month for an add-on US phone number, there is no phone-line support, and the unlimited tier ($48.99 in Thailand) is poor value next to Saily's own fixed plans. For a 1-2GB trip, nobody in this guide beats it on price.

Who should avoid it: heavy users, since mid-size plans are merely competitive rather than cheapest, and anyone needing a local (non-US) number.

4. Holiday.com - cheapest entry price with a genuine top-up model

Holiday.com, built by the ExpressVPN team as a standalone eSIM service with no VPN included, covers 150+ destinations with plans from $3.50, including Thailand. Two policies make it unusually budget-friendly in structure: a full year to activate after purchase, and a one-eSIM model where you install once and top up for every future trip instead of buying and deleting profiles. Its unlimited plans are also honestly labelled: 3GB per day at high speed, then unlimited data at 10Mbps, which is far more usable than the 1Mbps floors common elsewhere. Support is 24/7 live chat and email.

The gaps: it is the newest service here, its apps are still maturing, Android setup can require manual APN entry, and an independent VPNCompare review found mid-size plans less competitive in its test (10GB/30 days at $35) as well as non-local IP routing that blocked some region-locked apps in Thailand. Compare the full plan ladder for your destination before assuming the $3.50 entry price scales.

Who should avoid it: Android users who dislike fiddling with settings, and anyone who needs a local IP address for banking or local apps.

5. aloSIM - small, cheap and slightly limited

Canadian provider aloSIM, founded in 2022, undercuts bigger names at both ends of the Thai market: 1GB/7 days for $3.50 and 20GB/30 days for $19.50 on AIS, with day-based plans throttling to 1Mbps after 3GB of daily high-speed data. A free international phone number is bundled, which is rare at this price, and top-ups are available in the app. The limitations are scale-related: no global plan, fewer unlimited options and smaller maximum allowances than the market leaders. A solid pick for single-country trips; less so for multi-continent itineraries, where our guide to the best eSIM for multiple destinations covers stronger options.

6. Airalo - huge coverage, fading budget credentials

Airalo remains the biggest store, with 200+ locations, well-rated apps (4.7 on iOS, 4.6 on Android) and a "from $4.00" headline that still holds on regional plans such as Asia. But our country-level checks show the budget position weakening. Thailand now lists only unlimited plans from $9.50 for 3 days plus one 50GB/10-day Dtac plan with calls at $9.90, and Turkey sells nothing but unlimited, from $11.50 for 3 days to $72.50 for 30 days. That 30-day Turkey plan costs nine times MobiMatter's 12GB alternative, and fair-use throttling applies once daily allowances are consumed. The $9.90 Thai plan is excellent value at $0.20/GB and includes 100 minutes of calls, so Airalo can still win specific battles. It just no longer wins the war on price. Our full Airalo review covers the wider service.

A note on Yesim

Yesim, run by Genesis Group AG of Zug, Switzerland since 2019, deserves a mention for its Pay & Fly wallet: per-megabyte billing from a prepaid balance valid 12 months, which suits very light users who hate expiring bundles. We rank it below the main six because independent reviews describe overall pricing as mid-range, its free VPN is iOS-only, unlimited throttle thresholds are poorly documented, and Trustpilot has previously flagged and removed fake reviews for the brand. Approach with open eyes.

The six budget eSIM traps

  1. Tiny validity windows. A $3-4 plan usually carries 7 days of validity. Unused data is forfeited when the window closes, with no rollover, so a 1GB/7-day plan you half-use costs double per gigabyte.
  2. Expired-data forfeiture by auto-activation. Nomad plans auto-activate 60 days after purchase and Saily plans 30 days after, whether you have travelled or not. Buy early and your plan can expire before your flight. Holiday.com's 1-year window is the honourable exception.
  3. Top-ups priced above the first purchase. Introductory and sale prices frequently apply only to the initial plan; top-up menus are priced separately and rarely discounted. Check the top-up price list before buying, not after your data runs out mid-trip.
  4. "From" prices that exist in one place. Airalo's $4.00 headline is real, but not in Turkey (minimum $11.50) or Thailand (minimum $9.50). Holiday.com's $3.50 applies to entry plans, with larger allowances priced destination by destination. Always price your actual country.
  5. Throttled "unlimited" that loses to fixed data. Unlimited plans typically mean 1-3GB per day at full speed, then 512kbps-10Mbps. Airalo's $72.50 unlimited month in Turkey versus MobiMatter's $7.99 for 12GB shows how badly the maths can favour fixed data. Our eSIM reliability guide explains what throttled speeds actually feel like, and Holafly vs MobiMatter runs this exact unlimited-versus-fixed comparison in depth.
  6. Marketplace plans with hard refund rules. On MobiMatter, refunds require an unused, unactivated eSIM within its activation period, and processing fees may apply. Cheap is only cheap when it works first time.

Advantages and disadvantages of going budget

Advantages: genuine savings of 80-95% against roaming charges (see eSIM vs roaming); fixed-data plans force healthy Wi-Fi habits; marketplace competition keeps driving prices down; small starter plans let you trial a provider for under $4.

Disadvantages: budget plans are almost always data-only; support is chat and email at best; validity windows punish loose planning; the cheapest deals concentrate in popular destinations, so obscure countries stay expensive; and headline prices reward careless comparison.

Who should choose what

  • Shoestring travellers (every dollar counts): MobiMatter, if you read each listing's terms and buy close to departure. The Thailand and Turkey deals are unmatched.
  • Value-focused travellers (cheap, but predictable): Nomad for most trips, especially Asia-Pacific; Saily when you only need 1-3GB.
  • Light users on short city breaks: Saily's $2.99 Thai starter or aloSIM's $3.50 equivalent.
  • Frequent travellers who hate reinstalling: Holiday.com's one-eSIM top-up model with its 1-year activation window.
  • Data-hungry streamers: Airalo's 50GB Thai plan at $9.90, or a large MobiMatter allowance; skip throttled unlimited plans entirely.

The verdict

The verdict: Nomad is the best budget eSIM for 2026, because it combines near-lowest prices with terms you can actually read, and it is strongest exactly where budget travel concentrates, in Asia-Pacific. MobiMatter is the outright cheapest and the right choice for shoestring travellers who treat each listing's fine print as part of the price. Value-focused travellers with small data needs should default to Saily, whose sub-$3 starter plans and Nord Security ownership make it the safest cheap option. Holiday.com is the structural bargain to watch, and Airalo, for all its coverage, is no longer a budget pick in the countries where budgets matter most.

Frequently asked questions

What is the cheapest eSIM in 2026?

On raw price per gigabyte, MobiMatter marketplace deals lead: our research found Thailand at $0.20/GB (True 50GB/10 days, $9.99) on 18 July 2026. For the cheapest small plan, Saily's $2.99 for 1GB in Thailand and a $1.79 entry plan reported by Cybernews are the lowest we verified.

Is a cheap "unlimited" eSIM better value than a fixed data plan?

Usually not. Unlimited plans throttle after a daily allowance (commonly 1-3GB, then 512kbps-10Mbps), yet cost several times more than fixed plans. In Turkey, 30 days unlimited from Airalo cost $72.50 while 12GB from MobiMatter cost $7.99. Only heavy daily streamers come out ahead on unlimited.

Do budget eSIM plans expire if I do not use the data?

Yes. When the validity period ends, unused data is forfeited with no rollover. Some plans also auto-activate a set time after purchase (60 days at Nomad, 30 days at Saily), so the countdown can start before you travel.

Do cheap eSIMs include a phone number for calls and texts?

Almost never; budget eSIMs are data-only. Exceptions exist: aloSIM bundles a free international number, Saily sells a US number for $0.99/month, and Airalo's $9.90 Thai Dtac plan includes 100 call minutes. For voice, use data apps such as WhatsApp.

Are marketplace eSIMs like MobiMatter safe to buy?

The plans come from real operators such as 3, Orange and Telenor, and the discounts are genuine. The risk is procedural: terms, support and top-ups vary per listing, and refunds require an unused eSIM within its activation window, with possible processing fees. Careful buyers do well; careless ones have little recourse.
