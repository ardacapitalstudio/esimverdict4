Can You Keep Two eSIMs Active at the Same Time? Dual SIM in 2026

Yes - most recent phones run two eSIMs at once. Device-by-device dual SIM guide for iPhone, Samsung and Pixel, plus the travel setup that avoids roaming fees.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 13 min read

Yes - on most phones sold since 2021 you can keep two eSIMs active at the same time, and on almost anything with eSIM support you can keep one eSIM active alongside your physical SIM. Apple states that iPhone 13 and later support two simultaneously active eSIMs, Google says the same for the Pixel 7 onwards, and Samsung finally joined them with the Galaxy S25 series. Older eSIM phones still run one physical SIM and one eSIM together, which covers the classic travel setup of home number plus travel data.

The distinction that trips people up is active versus stored. Active lines are the ones registered to a network right now - ready to receive calls, texts and data. Stored eSIMs are just profiles sitting in the phone's eSIM chip, switched off until you need them. Apple says an iPhone can hold eight or more eSIMs while using two numbers at once; Android phones store several profiles too, though neither Google nor Samsung publishes a fixed figure.

This guide sets out what each device generation can do, then walks through the standard traveller configuration - home SIM for calls and banking codes, travel eSIM for data - including the settings that stop your home line quietly racking up roaming charges.

At a glance

  • Two active eSIMs at once: iPhone 13 and later (including iPhone SE 3rd generation), Pixel 7 and later, Samsung Galaxy S25 series.
  • One physical SIM + one eSIM active: iPhone XS/XR through iPhone 12, Pixel 3a through 6a, Galaxy S20 through S24, Z Flip/Fold and Note 20 - region and carrier permitting.
  • eSIM-only hardware: US-market iPhone 14 and later ship without a SIM tray; the US Pixel 10 range (except the 10 Pro Fold) is eSIM-only too.
  • Stored profiles: eight or more on iPhone per Apple; "several" on Samsung and Pixel. Only two lines can be active at once on any mainstream phone.
  • The catch: the phone uses one line at a time for mobile data. Dual SIM means two live numbers, not two simultaneous data connections.

Active versus stored: the numbers that actually matter

Think of the eSIM chip as a wallet: you can carry many cards but only pay with the ones you take out. Apple's documentation says a compatible iPhone stores eight or more eSIMs; Google and Samsung are vaguer, but travellers routinely park four or five profiles on Android flagships without trouble.

Active lines are capped at two everywhere - no mainstream phone offers triple standby. A frequent traveller can still store eSIMs for Europe, the US and Asia and toggle the right one on at each destination while the home line stays live throughout. One caution: many travel eSIMs cannot be reinstalled from the same QR code once deleted, so disable rather than delete - our recovering a deleted eSIM guide covers what can be salvaged.

What each brand supports

iPhone

Apple introduced eSIM with the iPhone XS, XS Max and XR in 2018. Those models through to the iPhone 12 run Dual SIM as one nano-SIM plus one eSIM; you can store several eSIMs but only use one at a time. The iPhone 13 family and the third-generation SE added "Dual SIM with two eSIMs", meaning two eSIM lines active together with no physical SIM involved.

In the United States, Apple went further: "iPhone 14 and iPhone 14 Plus remove the SIM tray for US models", as the launch announcement put it, and every US iPhone since has been eSIM-only. International iPhone 14, 15, 16 and 17 models keep the tray, so a non-US buyer can still combine a physical home SIM with a travel eSIM.

Samsung

Samsung has shipped eSIM in most flagships since the Galaxy S20, but always as one physical SIM plus one eSIM active - with more regional exceptions than its rivals. The S20 FE lacks eSIM in several markets, the S21 FE has it disabled on some carrier variants, and carrier-locked models often shipped with eSIM switched off. S23 and S24 owners confirmed in forum testing that two eSIMs cannot be made active together on those generations. The Galaxy S25 series is Samsung's first with genuine dual active eSIM support, alongside the retained nano-SIM slot.

Pixel

Google supports one physical SIM plus one eSIM from the Pixel 3a onwards, and two simultaneously active eSIMs from the Pixel 7 - with the caveat, per Google's documentation, that your carrier has to permit it. The Pixel 4a (5G) and later can connect to 5G while in dual SIM mode. Like Apple, Google has begun dropping the tray: US Pixel 10 models, apart from the 10 Pro Fold, are eSIM-only.

The standard travel setup, step by step

The configuration most travellers want is simple: home SIM stays active for calls, texts and one-time banking codes; travel eSIM handles all data. Data-only travel eSIMs from providers such as Airalo, Nomad and Saily are built for exactly this arrangement - see our budget eSIM comparison for current pricing.

On iPhone

  1. Before you fly, install the travel eSIM: Settings > Mobile Data > Add eSIM, then scan the QR code or use the provider's app. Label the lines clearly, for example "Personal" and "Travel".
  2. Set your default voice line to the home SIM under Settings > Mobile Data. Calls, SMS and two-factor codes keep arriving on your usual number.
  3. Once you land, set Mobile Data to the travel eSIM and turn OFF "Allow Mobile Data Switching". Left on, this lets the phone silently borrow data from your home line - which abroad means roaming charges.
  4. Open the home line's own settings and switch Data Roaming off. The line stays registered for calls and texts but can never pass data.
  5. Check both numbers are enabled under Settings > Messages > Send & Receive. Apple confirms both lines can use iMessage, FaceTime, SMS and MMS.

On Android

The flow is nearly identical, with menus in different places. On a Pixel: Settings > Network & internet > SIMs > Add eSIM, then set preferences for calls, texts and mobile data individually. On a Samsung: Settings > Connections > SIM Manager, add the eSIM, then use Preferred SIMs to pin calls and texts to the home line and mobile data to the travel eSIM. On both, open the home SIM's own settings and disable roaming for that line specifically.

One Android quirk worth knowing: Google notes that not every carrier combination cooperates in dual SIM mode, and forum reports describe RCS chat features attaching only to the primary SIM. Test messaging before you rely on it.

Will iMessage and WhatsApp still use my normal number?

Yes, and this is the main reason the dual SIM setup beats swapping SIMs entirely. iMessage registers against your phone numbers and Apple ID, and both active lines can be enabled at once, so conversations carry on exactly as at home even while every byte travels over the travel eSIM.

WhatsApp is simpler still. The account is tied to the number you verified, not to whichever SIM currently supplies data. Use a travel eSIM's data for a month and your WhatsApp number never changes; contacts notice nothing. The same logic applies to Signal, Telegram and most banking apps: they care about data connectivity, not which line provides it. The one wrinkle is verification SMS - if an app insists on re-verifying you, the code arrives on your home number, which is exactly why you keep that line active.

Battery: what dual standby really costs

Two active lines mean two network registrations, so some extra drain is unavoidable. With decent coverage the difference is modest - the second line sits in standby, waking only for paging signals. Drain becomes noticeable when one line has weak or no coverage, because the radio burns power hunting for a network; a home SIM with no roaming partner in a remote area will keep searching regardless.

The fix is simple: if you do not need the home line for a stretch, toggle it off - the profile stays stored and re-enables in seconds. Battery worries alone are no reason to avoid dual SIM; a hunting radio in a dead zone drains far more than a second registered line ever will.

Edge cases worth knowing

5G in dual SIM mode

Whatever the marketing implies, your phone uses one mobile data network at a time - Apple states this explicitly. Early 5G handsets went further: the iPhone 12 initially dropped to 4G whenever two lines were active until a 2021 software update, and Google only guarantees 5G in dual SIM mode from the Pixel 4a (5G) onwards. Some budget Android models still restrict 5G to whichever line carries data; heavy users should check their specific model, though 4G is rarely the bottleneck for maps and messaging.

Corporate phones that refuse a second eSIM

If your employer manages your handset, the Add eSIM option may be greyed out. Mobile device management profiles can block eSIM changes outright, and carrier-locked corporate fleets often restrict lines to the company operator. There is no safe workaround on the device itself - the restriction is policy, not a bug. Our eSIMs on corporate devices guide covers the options, from asking IT for an exception to carrying a cheap second handset.

Two travel eSIMs for coverage redundancy

Dual active eSIMs are not just for keeping a home number alive. Long-haul travellers increasingly run two travel eSIMs from different providers riding different local networks, switching the data line when one drops. Coverage failures usually hit one network at a time, so the second profile is cheap insurance - our eSIM reliability research explains how often plans fail and why. For a durable second number rather than throwaway data, a permanent second-line eSIM does the same job with calls and texts included.

Advantages and disadvantages

Advantages

  • Home number stays reachable for calls, SMS and two-factor codes while travel data costs a fraction of roaming - see eSIM vs roaming for the price gap.
  • iMessage, WhatsApp and banking apps carry on with your normal number.
  • Store many eSIMs and hop between destinations without touching a SIM tool - useful for multi-country trips.
  • Redundancy: a second line on a different network rescues you when the first has no signal.

Disadvantages

  • Only one line can carry data at a time on any phone.
  • Misconfigured settings - particularly data switching left on - can produce surprise roaming bills on the home line.
  • Modest extra battery drain, worse where one line has poor coverage.
  • Pre-2021 devices and the Galaxy S23/S24 cannot run two eSIMs together; corporate devices may block second profiles entirely.

Who should choose what

  • iPhone 13 or later, Pixel 7 or later, Galaxy S25: run home line plus travel eSIM as two eSIMs, or keep the physical SIM if you still have one. Full flexibility.
  • Older eSIM phones (iPhone XS-12, Galaxy S20-S24, Pixel 3a-6a): keep the home number on the physical SIM and install travel eSIMs digitally. The classic setup works perfectly.
  • US iPhone 14+ owners: your home line is already an eSIM; add travel profiles alongside it and switch the data line per trip.
  • Moving abroad rather than travelling: you will eventually want a local number too - our expat first-month guide covers bridging the gap while keeping home banking access alive.
  • Locked or managed phones: resolve the lock or MDM policy first; no travel eSIM will install until you do.

The verdict

Two active eSIMs at once is now a standard feature: iPhone 13 and later, Pixel 7 and later, and Samsung's S25 series all support it, and every eSIM phone back to 2018 handles the physical-plus-eSIM equivalent. The capability question matters less than the configuration. Set the home SIM as default voice line with data roaming off, put all data on the travel eSIM, and disable automatic data switching - those three settings deliver the entire benefit and remove the entire risk. Get them right and you keep every call, code and WhatsApp thread while paying local data prices. Leave data switching on and you fund your operator's next results presentation.

Frequently asked questions

How many eSIMs can I store on my phone?

Apple says an iPhone stores eight or more eSIM profiles. Samsung and Google do not publish figures, but several profiles fit comfortably on recent models. Storage is separate from use: at most two lines are active at once, and only one carries data at a time.

Will using a travel eSIM change my WhatsApp number?

No. WhatsApp stays registered to the number you verified, regardless of which SIM provides data. Your account, chats and contacts continue unchanged over the travel eSIM's connection. Avoid the in-app change-number option unless you genuinely want to move the account permanently.

Can I still receive calls and texts on my home number abroad?

Yes, as long as the home line stays enabled. Incoming SMS, including bank verification codes, generally arrives free of charge. Answering voice calls abroad is billed at your operator's roaming rates, so let non-urgent calls go to voicemail - and note that retrieving voicemail from abroad can itself incur charges on some networks.

Does running two lines drain the battery much faster?

The overhead of a second registered line is small in good coverage. Drain increases meaningfully when one line cannot find a network and keeps searching, which is common abroad on home SIMs with limited roaming partners. Disable the home line temporarily if you notice this, or accept a few percent for the security of staying reachable.

Why is Add eSIM greyed out on my work phone?

Company device management commonly blocks eSIM installation, and carrier-locked handsets refuse profiles from other operators. This is deliberate policy rather than a fault. Speak to your IT department, or use a separate personal device for travel - our corporate devices guide sets out the practical routes.
