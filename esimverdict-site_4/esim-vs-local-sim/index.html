eSIM vs Local SIM: Pros and Cons - Which Is Cheaper in 2026?

eSIM or local SIM abroad? We price both for Thailand, Spain and the USA, weigh queues, ID rules and scams, and give a decision framework by trip type.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 14 min read

Buy a local SIM at the airport or install a travel eSIM before you fly? Backpackers have argued about this for a decade, usually without numbers. So we priced both options for three popular destinations at a realistic allowance of roughly 15-20GB over 30 days, and the answer is less one-sided than either camp claims.

In Spain, the local SIM wins on price and it is not close: Vodafone's prepaid tier gives 50GB for €10, about a fifth of the per-gigabyte cost of a typical travel eSIM. In the USA the positions reverse, because American prepaid plans start at $45 a month before tax, while a 20GB travel eSIM costs $36.99. Thailand sits almost exactly at parity, with a 599 baht tourist SIM and a $19.99 travel eSIM landing within a dollar of each other.

Price is only half the argument, though. Local SIMs bring a local number, full-speed local routing and cheap top-ups; they also bring airport queues, passport registration, occasional biometric checks and the small but real risk of losing your home SIM from the tray. This guide works through both columns, then gives you a decision framework by trip type. If you are choosing between an eSIM and your carrier's roaming instead, that comparison lives in our eSIM vs roaming guide.

At a glance

  • Local SIMs win on price in cheap prepaid markets. Spain: 50GB for €10 (Vodafone). Thailand: 30GB for 599 baht (True tourist SIM). Travel eSIMs rarely beat $1 per GB; good local SIMs manage $0.20-$0.65.
  • Travel eSIMs win in expensive prepaid markets. US prepaid starts at about $45 a month plus taxes; a 20GB/30-day US travel eSIM costs $36.99 (Saily) and an unlimited 30-day plan $69 (Airalo).
  • Local SIMs give you a local number for restaurant bookings, ride-hailing apps and SMS verification; nearly all travel eSIMs are data-only.
  • Registration is the local SIM's hidden cost: passport registration in Thailand and Spain, face or fingerprint scans in Thailand, and a CPF tax number often demanded in practice in Brazil.
  • The eSIM's core advantage is sequencing: installed on your sofa, working before you clear immigration, home SIM still in the phone for banking codes.
  • The strongest play for long stays is hybrid: land on a travel eSIM, buy a local SIM in town during your first week.
  • Local carriers increasingly sell prepaid eSIMs of their own, which removes the physical swap risk while keeping local pricing.

Thailand: parity, with an asterisk

Thailand's tourist SIM market is fiercely competitive. True's tourist SIM lists 30GB for 30 days at 599 baht, AIS sells 50GB for 899 baht, and dtac's Happy Tourist range runs from 299 baht for 15GB over 8 days to 1,199 baht for 30 days of unlimited data. These bundle unlimited use of major social apps and some calling credit, and the operators' official counters sit right in the arrivals halls.

Travel eSIMs match this almost exactly. Saily's 20GB/30-day Thailand plan is $19.99, and Airalo sells a dtac-operated eSIM with 50GB and 100 local minutes for $9.90, albeit with only 10 days of validity. When the local networks themselves wholesale capacity to eSIM platforms this cheaply, the queue at the airport counter buys you very little.

Spain: the local SIM's showcase

Spain is what a cheap prepaid market looks like. The Prepaid Data SIM Card Wiki records Vodafone's prepaid tiers at €10-€40 for 50GB-200GB per 28 days with unlimited domestic calls, Movistar at €10-€20 for 10-35GB, and Orange's Mundo plans at €10-€20 for 10-35GB plus bonus data. At €10 for 50GB, Vodafone works out around $0.24 per gigabyte.

The best travel eSIM prices for Spain are Orange's own Travel eSIM at $16.99 for 20GB/30 days and Saily's 20GB at $22.99, roughly $0.85-$1.15 per gigabyte. That is fine value in absolute terms, and our best eSIM for Spain guide compares the field, but a traveller staying two weeks or more and using serious data is paying four to five times the local rate for convenience. Note the catch below on registration, though: since 2024, Spanish SIM registration has tightened, and Vodafone stores now require a passport or Spanish ID rather than any EU identity card.

USA: where the eSIM wins outright

The United States has no meaningful tourist SIM tradition and no cheap prepaid tier. T-Mobile's cheapest single-line prepaid plan with useful data is the Starter Monthly at $45 with AutoPay for 15GB, before state and local taxes, and you still need to find a store or order a SIM kit to a US address. Against that, Saily's 20GB/30-day US eSIM at $36.99 or Airalo's 30-day unlimited at $69 (on T-Mobile's network, capped after a daily full-speed allowance) is cheaper, and enormously less hassle. Our best eSIM for the USA guide ranks the options in detail.

Where local SIMs win

Price per gigabyte. In most of Asia, Latin America and southern Europe, local prepaid data costs a fraction of travel eSIM rates. Spain's €10 for 50GB and Thailand's 599 baht for 30GB set the benchmark; even our best budget eSIM picks rarely get under $1 per GB for a single country.

A local number. Restaurant booking platforms, ride-hailing apps, delivery services and ticketing sites frequently insist on a local mobile number for SMS verification. Travel eSIMs are data-only in nearly every destination, so a local SIM is the only clean answer if your trip depends on those services.

Full-speed local routing. A local SIM connects you natively: local IP address, local content servers, no detours. Many travel eSIMs route your traffic through a breakout gateway in another country, which can add latency, confuse streaming geolocation and trip regional pricing on websites. We explain the mechanics in our guide to how eSIM traffic routing works.

No daily speed caps. Local unlimited plans, such as dtac's 1,199 baht Happy Tourist Infinite, are typically unthrottled. Travel eSIM "unlimited" plans almost always slow you after a daily allowance of 1-5GB, a distinction we unpack in our reliability guide.

Cheap top-ups and longevity. Local SIMs recharge at any convenience store, and can often be kept alive for return trips.

Where local SIMs lose

Airport queues and steered spending. After a long-haul flight, the operator counters at a major arrivals hall can mean a 20-40 minute wait, and staff understandably promote the dearer tourist bundles. Independent kiosks and resellers add their own markup on top of list prices. City-centre operator shops and convenience stores are usually the better deal, but that means a day or two offline first.

ID, registration and biometrics. Almost every country now requires SIM registration. Thailand requires your passport and, under rules in force since 2017, operators verify buyers biometrically with a face or fingerprint scan. Spain requires a passport or Spanish ID, and since 2024 Vodafone no longer accepts other EU national identity cards. Brazil is the awkward standout: operators are supposed to accept a passport, but customer reports collected on the prepaid wiki suggest many shops still demand a CPF tax number in practice, which is why we walk through the workarounds in our best eSIM for Brazil guide. A handful of markets go further still and fingerprint all buyers.

Language barriers. Registering a SIM, choosing a package and fixing a problem all happen in the local language once you leave the tourist counters.

The physical swap. Ejecting your home SIM means carrying a loose nano-SIM around for weeks. Lose it and you lose your number, and with it every banking one-time passcode, until your home carrier replaces it. Phones with eSIM support avoid the problem entirely by running two lines at once, as our guide to using two eSIMs at once explains.

Scams and duds. Common traveller complaints include pre-used or improperly registered SIMs that get suspended days later, "30-day" packages that turn out to be shorter promotional packs, and unlimited plans throttled harder than advertised. Buying from official operator stores avoids most of this.

The eSIM convenience case

The travel eSIM's pitch is sequencing, not price. You buy the plan at home, install it on your own Wi-Fi with the provider's English-language instructions, and data starts the moment your plane's wheels touch down. There is no queue, no counter, no registration form and no language barrier, and support is a 24/7 chat rather than a shop across town.

Just as important, your home SIM never leaves the phone. Set the eSIM as your data line, switch data roaming off on the home line, and banking codes and important texts keep arriving at no cost while the eSIM carries your traffic. You also fix your spend up front, in your own currency, with a refund policy you can read in advance rather than a kiosk receipt. The trade-offs are real but known: no local number, breakout routing on some plans, and per-gigabyte prices that a cheap prepaid market will beat.

The hybrid strategy for long stays

For stays of a month or more, the smart answer is both. Land on a modest travel eSIM, 5-10GB, enough for maps, messaging and admin during your first few days. Then, once you are rested and in town, walk into an official operator store with your passport and buy the local SIM or local eSIM at list price, in daylight, with time to compare packages. You skip the airport premium and the jet-lagged queue, but you are never offline. We set this playbook out in detail in our guide to the best eSIM for an expat's first month.

Local carrier eSIMs: the gap is closing

The neatest development of the past two years is local operators selling prepaid eSIMs directly to visitors, which combines local pricing with eSIM convenience. Where tourists can actually get them today:

  • Thailand: AIS, True and dtac all sell tourist eSIMs through their official websites and airport counters, at the same prices as the physical tourist SIMs. Passport verification still applies.
  • Spain: Orange sells prepaid eSIMs online via its tuesim.es channel (EU passport or ID required, activation in Spain), and its Orange Travel eSIM, 20GB for 30 days at $16.99, is sold internationally with no store visit at all.
  • USA: T-Mobile's prepaid plans, including the $45 Starter, can be activated as an eSIM through its website and prepaid app, no store required.

Expect this column to grow. When a local carrier will sell you an eSIM online at domestic prices, the traditional travel eSIM has to compete on service, multi-country coverage and simplicity rather than on queue avoidance alone.

Advantages and disadvantages

Local SIM: advantages. Cheapest data per gigabyte in most of the world; a local number for calls, SMS and app verification; native local routing with no breakout quirks; genuinely unthrottled unlimited options; top-ups everywhere.

Local SIM: disadvantages. Queues and steered selling at airports; passport registration, biometric checks and, in Brazil's case, frequent CPF demands; language barriers off the tourist path; the risk of losing your ejected home SIM; scam and suspension risk outside official stores.

Travel eSIM: advantages. Working before you land; home SIM stays in for banking codes; English-language purchase and 24/7 support; fixed cost in your own currency; no physical anything to lose.

Travel eSIM: disadvantages. Two to five times the local price per gigabyte in cheap markets; data-only, so no local number; "unlimited" plans carry daily speed caps; some plans route traffic through another country; requires an eSIM-capable, unlocked phone.

Who should choose what

  • Short-trip travellers and first-timers: travel eSIM, without much deliberation. The savings a local SIM offers on a one-week trip rarely clear $10.
  • Anyone visiting the USA: travel eSIM. The local prepaid market is priced against you.
  • Budget travellers spending 2+ weeks in one cheap-prepaid country: local SIM, bought at an official store in town rather than the airport.
  • Travellers who need a local number: local SIM, or a local carrier eSIM where available.
  • Long-stay travellers, expats and workations: hybrid, with the eSIM covering arrival week.
  • Owners of eSIM-free or locked phones: local SIM is your only real alternative to roaming.

The verdict

The classic backpacker answer, "always buy local, tourists who pay for eSIMs are mugs", was written when the price gap was tenfold. It is not any more. Thailand's tourist SIM and a 20GB travel eSIM now cost the same $19-$20, the USA has flipped decisively to the eSIM, and only in classically cheap prepaid markets such as Spain does the local SIM retain a commanding price lead, at €10 for 50GB against roughly $17-$23 for 20GB.

Our framework is simple. Under two weeks in one country, or any trip to the USA, buy the travel eSIM and spend your first hour abroad outdoors rather than in a queue. Over two weeks in a cheap prepaid market, or whenever you need a local number, the local SIM earns its paperwork, ideally as a local eSIM so your home SIM never leaves the phone. For a month or more, run the hybrid: eSIM to land on, local SIM by week one. Whatever you choose, price the trip in usable gigabytes rather than headline claims; our budget eSIM ranking shows how quickly "cheap" and "cheapest per GB" diverge.

Frequently asked questions

Is a local SIM always cheaper than a travel eSIM?

No. It is usually cheaper in Asia, Latin America and southern Europe, sometimes dramatically so, as with Vodafone Spain's 50GB for €10. In the USA the reverse holds: prepaid plans start around $45 a month plus taxes, while 20GB travel eSIMs cost about $37. In Thailand the two now cost roughly the same.

Do I need my passport to buy a local SIM abroad?

Almost always, yes. SIM registration is law in most countries: Thailand scans your passport and verifies buyers biometrically, Spain requires a passport or Spanish ID, and Brazilian shops frequently ask for a CPF tax number even though passports are officially accepted. Travel eSIMs bought from international providers generally need no identity documents at all, though a few destinations require ID for eSIMs too.

Can I keep my home number while using a local SIM?

If your phone supports dual SIM, yes: keep the home SIM in its slot (or as an eSIM), add the local SIM, and set the local line for data. If you must physically eject your home SIM, store it somewhere safe, since losing it means losing SMS banking codes until it is replaced. Buying the local carrier's eSIM instead of plastic avoids the problem entirely.

Can tourists buy eSIMs directly from local carriers?

Increasingly, yes. AIS, True and dtac sell tourist eSIMs in Thailand at physical-SIM prices, Orange sells Spanish prepaid eSIMs online and an internationally available Orange Travel eSIM, and T-Mobile US activates prepaid eSIMs through its app. Registration requirements still apply where local law demands them.

Why does my travel eSIM show an IP address in another country?

Many travel eSIMs are technically roaming products, with your traffic breaking out to the internet through a gateway in the provider's home hub, often Singapore, Hong Kong or an EU country. That can affect streaming libraries, search results and latency. A local SIM always gives you a local IP.
