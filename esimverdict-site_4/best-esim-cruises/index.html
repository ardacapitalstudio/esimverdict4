Best eSIM for Cruise Ships and Ferry Travel 2026: What Works at Sea

Standard travel eSIMs fail at sea. Learn how ship networks work, which providers offer real maritime cover, and the port-day strategy that saves money.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 13 min read

Here is the uncomfortable truth that most eSIM marketing skips: almost no travel eSIM works once your ship leaves the coast. Cruise ships carry their own onboard cellular networks, run by specialists such as Wireless Maritime Services (WMS) and Cellular at Sea, which route traffic over satellite once the vessel reaches international waters. Standard travel eSIMs from Airalo, Holafly, Nomad and nearly every other provider exclude these maritime networks entirely, so your phone simply shows no service - or worse, your home SIM connects and bills you at satellite rates that have reached €16.98 per megabyte.

Our research for this guide found exactly one mainstream consumer eSIM with genuine at-sea coverage: GigSky, whose cruise plans roam onto the ship's cellular network on more than 290 vessels. Everything else sold as a "cruise eSIM", including Holafly's cruise-branded plans, is really a port-day product that works on land and stops at the shoreline.

That does not make those products useless. The smart strategy for most cruisers in 2026 is a combination: ship wifi (now Starlink-backed on every major line) for sea days, plus a cheap regional eSIM for port days, with a maritime eSIM reserved for people who genuinely need mobile data mid-ocean. This guide explains how the technology works, what everything costs, and which setup fits each type of cruise - including ferries, which follow different rules altogether.

At a glance

  1. GigSky - best overall for cruises. The only mainstream eSIM with real maritime coverage, on 290+ ships plus 200+ land destinations, from around $5 per day.
  2. Ship wifi (Starlink) - best for sea days. Roughly $20-30 per device per day on major lines; the only realistic option for streaming and video calls mid-ocean.
  3. Holafly cruise plans - best for unlimited port-day data. Region-tailored unlimited plans from $11.90, but coverage is explicitly land only.
  4. Airalo - best budget port-day option. Regional packs from about $4, unlimited Europe plans from $11.50 for 3 days; no maritime coverage at all.
  5. Home carrier day pass - fallback for US travellers who need their own number at sea. AT&T and Verizon both charge about $20 per day on ships.

Rankings weigh price, coverage, network quality, plan flexibility, transparency, ease of use, hotspot support and customer service, with maritime coverage weighted heavily because it is the point of this guide.

How ship connectivity actually works

The ship's own cellular network

Once a cruise ship sails roughly 12 nautical miles offshore, land-based mobile towers drop away. The ship then switches on its own onboard cellular network, operated by a maritime specialist rather than any national carrier. Cellular at Sea, the largest operator, confirms that its network appears on phones as "cellularatsea", "wmsatsea", "NOR-18" or "901-18", and that all traffic runs over a satellite connection. If your home carrier has a roaming agreement with the maritime operator, your phone connects automatically, with billing handled by your carrier at whatever rate it chooses.

This is why maritime roaming is so dangerous. There is no EU-style price cap at sea - Brussels' roam-like-at-home rules explicitly do not apply on ferries and cruise ships, even in European waters, as consumer watchdogs at Alertify have documented. Their reporting cites satellite rates of €16.98 per megabyte, which turns a three-megabyte web page into a £45 mistake. Among US carriers, AT&T charges $2.05 per MB pay-as-you-go at sea, and T-Mobile offers no shipboard data at all, just voice at $5.99 per minute.

Starlink changed ship wifi, not ship cellular

Between 2022 and early 2026, every major cruise brand completed fleetwide Starlink installations, and coverage-focused outlets report the rollout finished across the biggest lines by early 2026. Ship wifi is now genuinely usable: fast enough for streaming and video calls in mid-ocean stretches that had nothing three years ago. Prices rose with quality. In 2026, Royal Caribbean's unified Surf + Stream tier runs about $20 per device per day bought in advance, Carnival's plans span roughly $20-26 per person per day, and Disney charges $30 for basic and $49 for streaming wifi per device per day.

The crucial nuance: Starlink feeds the ship's wifi, not its cellular network. Your eSIM does not benefit from Starlink. An eSIM either roams onto the maritime cellular network (almost none do) or it does nothing until you are near land.

Why your travel eSIM shows no service

Travel eSIMs resell capacity on land-based networks in each covered country. Maritime networks are a separate, expensive wholesale market that mainstream providers simply exclude. Airalo's own cruise guidance is candid about this, telling readers that once you are out in open waters "it's a no-go" and steering them towards ship wifi and offline downloads. Holafly's cruise product pages state plainly that coverage is provided "only on land" and is not guaranteed at sea. Our research across provider documentation found no maritime coverage from Airalo, Holafly, Nomad, Yesim, Saily, aloSIM or MobiMatter's marketplace - which is consistent with independent database esimdb's finding that GigSky is currently alone in bundling Cellular at Sea access into a consumer eSIM.

GigSky: the only true maritime eSIM

GigSky's cruise plans are the exception, and they are built specifically for this problem. The provider states coverage on more than 290 cruise ships across 20+ lines, including Carnival, Royal Caribbean, Disney, MSC, Norwegian, Princess, Celebrity, Holland America and P&O, combined with land coverage in 200+ destinations. Plans come in Cruise + Land regional bundles (Americas/Caribbean, Europe, Asia Pacific, Middle East and World variants) so the same eSIM works on deck and in port, and the maritime side activates once the ship is about 12 nautical miles offshore.

Pricing is the catch, because satellite capacity is not cheap. GigSky advertises cruise plans "as low as $5/day", and independent tracker BetterSIM logged representative prices of $18.99 for 512MB over one day, $28.04 for 1GB over seven days, $65.99 for 5GB over 30 days and $115.49 for 10GB over 30 days. An unlimited option throttles after 3.5GB of fast data per day. A free 100MB trial with no credit card lets you confirm your ship is covered before paying, which we rate highly for transparency. Treat GigSky data as a messaging, email and maps allowance rather than a streaming service, and it is very good value against $2.05-per-MB roaming. For heavier use at sea, ship wifi remains the better buy - and for a sense of how GigSky's everyday land plans stack up, see our guide to hidden eSIM costs.

Port days: where normal eSIMs earn their keep

A seven-night Mediterranean itinerary might touch Spain, France, Italy and Greece; a Caribbean loop can hit four island nations plus a US home port. Single-country eSIMs are a poor fit, so use a regional plan that covers the whole route - our multi-destination eSIM guide covers the mechanics in depth.

Holafly's cruise-branded plans are honest port-day products once you read the small print. The Mediterranean cruise eSIM covers eight countries including Spain, France, Italy, Greece, Croatia, Malta and Tunisia with unlimited data from $11.90 for 3 days to $73.90 for 30 days; the Caribbean version covers nine countries from $18.90 for 3 days. Both allow 1GB per day of hotspot sharing and both state that coverage applies on land only. Note the exclusions: Holafly's Caribbean plan does not cover private islands such as CocoCay or Ocean Cay, nor Freeport. Fair use kicks in above 90GB per month.

Airalo remains the budget pick for lighter users, with regional packs from about $4 for 1GB and a Europe plan spanning 42 countries, now including unlimited options from $11.50 for 3 days. Read our full Airalo review for the fine print, and remember port calls are short - most travellers need far less data than they think. Cheap regional options are compared further in our budget eSIM ranking.

One warning either way: buy and install the eSIM before you board, while you still have reliable land internet. Activation mid-cruise over paid ship wifi is an avoidable frustration, and eSIM reliability issues are much easier to solve on shore, as our eSIM reliability guide explains.

Ferries follow different rules

Ferry travel is more forgiving than cruising, but the trap is identical. On short coastal crossings - Greek island hops, Dover-Calais, most Adriatic and Baltic routes - your phone often stays within reach of land towers for much of the journey, so an ordinary regional eSIM keeps working. Island-hoppers heading through the Aegean can rely on a normal Greek or European plan for most of the trip; see our best eSIM for Greece guide.

Longer crossings are the danger zone. Overnight ferries to Spain, Ireland-France routes and open-sea stretches carry the same satellite-backed onboard networks as cruise ships, and the EU roaming cap does not apply once you connect to them. Ferry operators themselves warn passengers about mid-channel satellite charges. The discipline is simple: when the coastline drops from view, switch on airplane mode, or at minimum disable data roaming and lock your data line to your travel eSIM, which will fail safely to no service rather than roam expensively. The wider trade-offs between roaming and travel eSIMs are covered in our eSIM vs roaming comparison.

Advantages and disadvantages

GigSky cruise plans Pros: genuine at-sea coverage, land coverage bundled, free 100MB trial, no bill shock. Cons: expensive per GB, speeds constrained by satellite backhaul, overkill if ship wifi is already in your fare.

Ship wifi Pros: Starlink speeds, unlimited tiers, works mid-ocean. Cons: $20-49 per device per day, per-device pricing punishes families, useless once you step off the gangway.

Holafly cruise plans Pros: unlimited port-day data, one eSIM for a whole multi-country itinerary, 24/7 support. Cons: no at-sea coverage despite the cruise branding, hotspot capped at 1GB per day, private-island exclusions.

Airalo and other standard eSIMs Pros: cheapest way to cover ports, huge regional choice. Cons: nothing at sea, fixed packs can run dry mid-trip.

Who should choose what

  • Caribbean cruisers from a US home port: ship wifi for sea days plus Holafly's Caribbean plan or an Airalo regional pack for islands; add a US eSIM if you have pre-cruise days in Florida.
  • Mediterranean cruisers: port-heavy itineraries mean sea-day coverage matters less; a Holafly Mediterranean plan or Airalo Europe unlimited covers almost the whole trip, with ship wifi optional.
  • Transatlantic and repositioning cruises: many consecutive sea days make ship wifi near-essential; add GigSky if you want cellular data as backup or need to stay reachable off-wifi.
  • Workers afloat: buy the streaming wifi tier and carry GigSky as redundancy; neither alone is dependable enough for a critical video call.
  • Ferry passengers: a normal regional eSIM plus airplane-mode discipline mid-crossing; GigSky and ship wifi are rarely worth it on crossings under a day.

The verdict

The verdict: GigSky wins this category almost by default, because it is the only mainstream eSIM that actually works at sea - but the best-value setup for most cruisers is ship wifi on sea days combined with a cheap regional eSIM for ports. Holafly's cruise plans are well-designed port-day products with misleading shelf names, Airalo is the budget port option, and your home SIM should stay in airplane mode from the moment the coast disappears. Nobody should pay satellite roaming rates in 2026; every alternative on this page is cheaper.

Frequently asked questions

Do normal travel eSIMs work on cruise ships?

Only while the ship is docked or close to shore. Once you are roughly 12 nautical miles out, coverage switches to the ship's satellite-backed cellular network, which standard travel eSIMs from Airalo, Holafly, Nomad and others do not include. GigSky's cruise plans are the main consumer exception.

Why is data at sea so expensive?

Every byte travels via satellite to a maritime operator such as Wireless Maritime Services or Cellular at Sea, then through your carrier's billing. With no regulation at sea - EU roaming caps do not apply - carriers charge what they like, up to several euros or dollars per megabyte.

Is ship wifi worth buying now that it uses Starlink?

For sea days, usually yes. Major lines completed Starlink rollouts by early 2026 and streaming tiers genuinely support video calls. Expect $20-30 per device per day on most lines, more on Disney, and buy pre-cruise for the best rate.

Will my phone rack up charges without me noticing?

It can. If your home carrier has a maritime roaming agreement, your phone connects automatically to networks named cellularatsea, wmsatsea, NOR-18 or 901-18. Enable airplane mode when leaving port and use wifi only, or lock data to a travel eSIM that excludes maritime networks.

What about ferries between EU countries?

Close to shore you will usually stay on land networks, where EU roam-like-at-home rules protect EU SIM holders. Mid-crossing, ferries switch to onboard satellite networks that sit outside the EU cap, so the same airplane-mode discipline applies on longer routes.
