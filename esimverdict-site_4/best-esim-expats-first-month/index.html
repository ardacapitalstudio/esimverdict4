Best eSIM for Expats During Their First Month Overseas (2026)

Landed but no local number yet? We compare 30-day eSIM plans and phone-number options from Airalo, Holafly, Ubigi and more to bridge your first month abroad.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 14 min read

The best eSIM for expats during their first month overseas is not the same as the best eSIM for a holiday. You have just landed with two suitcases, no local number, and a to-do list that runs through maps, banking apps, flat viewings and government appointments. A local contract would be cheaper, but in most countries you cannot sign one yet: carriers want residence permits, local bank accounts or registered addresses that take weeks to obtain. What you need is a bridge - a large-allowance 30-day plan that works the moment you clear passport control, ideally with a phone number attached.

Our research points to a two-part answer. For the bridge month itself, Airalo's Discover+ global eSIM is the strongest all-rounder because it is the only mainstream option with a genuine phone number that handles both calls and texts, and it can sit alongside a cheap local data pack on the same phone. Holafly's monthly subscription is the simplest single product, pairing unlimited data with a number on its top tier, while Ubigi's $19 monthly 20GB plan is the value pick for Europe-bound movers. Nomad and Saily supply the cheapest one-off 30-day allowances if you only need data.

Just as important is the exit plan. Below we set out what local carriers in Spain, Germany, the UAE and Thailand actually demand before they will sell you a contract, and why staying on a travel eSIM beyond month two quietly becomes one of the most expensive mistakes an expat can make.

Why the first month abroad is different from tourism

A tourist needs data. A new arrival needs data plus proof of reachability. Estate agents want a number they can ring back. Utility companies, delivery services, medical practices and government appointment systems in many countries confirm bookings by SMS. Banks use text messages to verify new devices. Nearly every travel eSIM on the market is data-only: no number, no SMS, no way to receive any of it.

This is the trap of the first month. You cannot get a local number without paperwork, and you cannot easily complete the paperwork without a number. WhatsApp and email cover some of the gap, but a surprising amount of everyday admin in Spain, Germany, the UAE and Thailand still assumes you can receive a text or answer a call.

Three travel eSIM products currently address this directly:

  • Airalo Discover+ includes an international phone number based in the United States (+1) with real calls and texts in both directions. Airalo's help centre confirms the number works worldwide, though call forwarding and voicemail are not supported. Because the number is American, local services that insist on a domestic number may still reject it, but SMS verification from international platforms generally works.
  • Holafly Plans, the subscription version of Holafly, added a phone number to its Unlimited tier for subscriptions started on or after 4 November 2025. Holafly's own FAQ describes it as supporting inbound SMS and VoIP-based calling, so traditional incoming mobile calls are not part of the deal. TechRadar's launch coverage in December 2025 highlighted two-factor codes and local reservations as the target use cases.
  • Yesim sells virtual numbers as an add-on from roughly $3.00 per month in six countries including the US and UK. They receive inbound SMS only, and independent reviews note that some banking apps flag and reject VoIP-issued numbers, so treat this as a convenience rather than a guarantee.

None of these replaces a local number for every purpose. They are bridges, and judged as bridges they work.

Monthly subscriptions versus one-off packs

One-off packs from Nomad, Saily and Yesim are exactly what they sound like: the data expires after 30 days and nothing renews. That is safe but leaves you buying again from scratch, sometimes at a different price.

Subscription and auto-renewing products behave more like a phone plan. Holafly Plans renews every 30 days with no commitment and can be cancelled from the user profile; it also parks 1GB of backup data on your account in case you overrun. Ubigi's monthly plans renew automatically, reset the allowance each cycle and allow top-ups, though Ubigi states cancellation becomes possible after three months, which is worth knowing before you commit. Airalo's Discover+ packages auto-renew on their cycle and the eSIM itself is rechargeable indefinitely.

For a move, the subscription model fits better than it does for tourism. You do not know whether the residency paperwork will take four weeks or ten, and a renewable plan absorbs that uncertainty. The discipline it demands is remembering to cancel the moment your local contract goes live.

The handoff: from travel eSIM to local contract

The travel eSIM is month one. The local contract is the destination, because nothing beats domestic pricing. What stands between the two is paperwork, and it varies sharply by country.

  • Spain. Every SIM sale requires identity registration, but a passport is enough for prepaid. A contract (postpaid) is another matter: carriers ask for an NIE (foreigner identification number), a passport and normally a Spanish IBAN for direct debit. Once you have those, prices collapse - Digi sells 25GB for €5.00, 50GB for €7.00 and unlimited data for €10.00 per month.
  • Germany. Registration is mandatory for prepaid, contract and eSIM alike, verified by VideoIdent, PostIdent or in a shop with a passport, EU ID card or residence permit. Prepaid will often accept a temporary address, but contracts effectively require your registered German address (Anmeldung) and a German bank account.
  • UAE. Tourist prepaid SIMs run on a passport and visa and last up to 28 days. Anything longer term, prepaid or postpaid, requires a valid Emirates ID or residence visa, which new arrivals typically wait several weeks to receive.
  • Thailand. Foreigners register SIMs in person with a passport, and 2026 rules cap registrations at three SIMs per operator per person. Registered SIMs must be activated within 60 days or re-verification is required.

The pattern is consistent: prepaid is reachable within days of arrival, contracts wait on residency documents. A sensible sequence is travel eSIM on landing, local prepaid SIM once you can visit a shop with your passport, and a contract when the residence permit and bank account exist. Keep your home number alive for banking 2FA

Whatever you do abroad, do not let your home-country number die. Banks, brokers, tax portals and older services back home will keep sending one-time passcodes to it for years, and recovering access without that number ranges from tedious to impossible.

The standard approach is dual SIM: your home SIM (or ported eSIM) stays installed with roaming data switched off, so it can receive SMS while your travel eSIM carries all data. Receiving texts abroad is free on most networks, though always confirm your home carrier's policy and any inactivity rules that could see a quiet prepaid number recycled. Some expats instead port the number to a cheap VoIP-style holding service before departure. Our two eSIMs at once guide covers the setup, and our permanent second line guide looks at long-term options for keeping a number alive indefinitely.

The cost trap of staying on a travel eSIM

Travel eSIMs are priced for trips, not for lives. Holafly's Unlimited subscription at $64.90 per month is superb value for a bridge and dreadful value as a permanent arrangement: twelve months costs about $779, against roughly €120 for a year of Digi's unlimited plan in Spain. Even the cheap data-only packs lose: Nomad's excellent $35.00 for 50GB still runs to $420 a year, four to six times a Spanish or German budget contract, and you get no local number, no EU roaming rights and no customer standing with a domestic carrier.

There are quieter costs too. Travel eSIM traffic sometimes routes through foreign IP addresses, which can trip fraud checks on local services, and data-only plans leave you permanently borrowing other people's numbers for verification. Our hidden costs of cheap eSIMs guide catalogues these traps, and the eSIM versus roaming comparison covers the parallel question for shorter stays. Treat the travel eSIM as scaffolding: essential during construction, absurd to live in.

The verdict

For the first month overseas, buy the bridge, not the destination. Airalo Discover+ is our overall pick because reachability is the scarce resource in a move, and it is the only travel eSIM here that delivers a genuinely two-way phone number; run it beside a cheap 20GB-50GB data pack from Nomad, Saily or Ubigi and your total stays under $60 for the month. Then execute the handoff: prepaid local SIM with your passport in week one or two, full contract once the NIE, Anmeldung, Emirates ID or their local equivalent exists, home number kept alive on dual SIM throughout. The expats who overpay are the ones still renewing a travel eSIM in month six.

Frequently asked questions

Can I sign a local phone contract as soon as I arrive in a new country?

Usually not. Spain wants an NIE and typically a Spanish IBAN, Germany wants a registered address and generally a German bank account, and the UAE requires an Emirates ID or residence visa for anything beyond a 28-day tourist SIM. Prepaid SIMs registered against your passport are the fast route while that paperwork is pending.

Which travel eSIMs include a real phone number?

Airalo Discover+ includes a US-based +1 number with two-way calls and texts. Holafly's Unlimited subscription tier includes a number that receives SMS and makes VoIP calls. Yesim sells inbound-SMS virtual numbers from about $3.00 per month. Almost everything else on the travel eSIM market is data-only.

Will an eSIM phone number work for bank SMS verification?

Often, but not always. International platforms generally deliver codes to Airalo's +1 number without fuss. Some banks and government systems detect VoIP-issued numbers, which is a known limitation of virtual number services such as Yesim's, and some local services insist on a domestic number outright. Keep your home SIM active for existing accounts and switch new local services to a local number as soon as you have one.

How much data do I need for my first month abroad?

More than a holiday. Between flat-searching with the hotspot on, video calls home, maps and no home broadband, 20GB is a sensible floor and 50GB is comfortable. At current prices that is $21.00 to $35.00 with Nomad in Spain, or unlimited from $48.99 with Saily and $64.90 with Holafly's subscription.

Should I keep my old number when moving abroad?

Yes. Banking and tax two-factor codes will follow your old number for years, so keep the SIM or ported eSIM alive in your phone's second slot with roaming data off, or move the number to a holding service. Check your home carrier's inactivity policy so a prepaid number is not silently recycled.
