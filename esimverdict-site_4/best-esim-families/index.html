Best eSIM for Families Travelling Together (2026): One Plan or One Each?

One shared travel eSIM or one per device? We run real cost maths for a family of four, compare hotspot limits and rank the best family eSIM options for 2026.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 14 min read

Some links on eSIMVerdict are affiliate links. If you buy through them we may earn a commission at no extra cost to you. Commercial relationships never affect rankings or conclusions; our assessments are based on independent research.

Connecting a family abroad comes down to one decision: buy a single generous plan and share it by hotspot, or put an individual eSIM on every device. The travel eSIM industry has quietly made that choice harder than it should be, because the fine print on hotspot sharing varies enormously between providers. Holafly caps sharing at 1GB per day on its single-trip plans, which sinks the shared-plan strategy for most families, while Airalo states in writing that there is no tethering limit at all.

Our research for this guide priced a 10-day trip to Spain for two parents and two children across seven providers, and the result surprised us. Individual eSIMs with sensible data allowances cost barely more than one premium unlimited plan, and they remove every practical headache of tethering: the drained parent battery, the requirement to stay within Wi-Fi range of one phone, and the daily cap that arrives mid-afternoon. For most families, one eSIM each is the right answer, and it can cost as little as $52 for all four people.

There are exceptions. Families with very young children who carry no phones of their own, families with hand-me-down handsets that lack eSIM support, and parents who want one bill and one app to manage will each want a different setup. This guide covers all of them, ranked using our standard criteria: price, coverage, plan flexibility, transparency, ease of use, hotspot support and customer service.

  • Best shared single plan: Airalo unlimited - no tethering restrictions, stated in writing, 3GB/day at full speed

The two strategies in detail

Strategy one: one big plan shared by hotspot

The theory is appealing. Buy a single unlimited plan for one parent's phone, switch on the personal hotspot, and let the other three devices connect as if it were hotel Wi-Fi. One purchase, one installation, one thing to manage.

In practice this strategy stands or falls on the provider's tethering policy, and the differences are stark. Airalo's fair use policy states: "You can also use your device as a personal hotspot to share your connection with other devices. There is no limit on tethering or the number of devices you connect." The constraint is the plan's own speed threshold - unlimited plans run at full speed for 3GB per day, then drop to 1 Mbps until midnight local time. Shared between four people, 3GB of fast data evaporates quickly.

Holafly, the provider most people associate with unlimited data, is far more restrictive. Its Spain plans state that you can "share 1 GB of data per day with family, friends, or fellow travelers". On the device itself the data is unlimited, but the other three family members are collectively living on 1GB a day. A single hour of video calls home would consume most of it. Holafly's monthly subscription product does advertise unlimited hotspot use at $64.90 per month, but that is a different product aimed at long-stay travellers, not a 10-day family holiday. Our Holafly vs Airalo comparison covers this gap in more depth.

Nomad permits hotspot use on most plans and publishes no specific cap, though its help centre warns that stability "may vary by device or network", and its unlimited plans throttle after 2GB of fast data per day. Holiday.com's unlimited plans offer the most generous stated fallback of the big names: 3GB per day at high speed, then unlimited data at 10 Mbps, which is still fast enough for standard-definition streaming on a shared connection.

Even with a generous provider, the practical drawbacks remain. The hotspot phone's battery drains roughly twice as fast. Everyone must stay within about ten metres of the parent carrying the connection, which collapses the moment a teenager wants to stay at the pool while you visit a museum. And if that one phone fails, is lost or is stolen, the whole family is offline at once - a scenario our eSIM reliability guide suggests planning for.

Strategy two: an eSIM on every device

Individual eSIMs cost more upfront but remove every one of those failure modes. Each person's connection is independent, the family can split up freely, and a lost phone is an inconvenience rather than a blackout.

The cost gap is smaller than most families assume. As the table shows, four Saily plans sized to realistic usage cost $51.96 for ten days in Spain - about $19 more than a single shared Airalo unlimited plan, and $15 more than the Holafly plan whose hotspot allowance would have made the trip miserable anyway. Fixed-data plans from the providers in our budget eSIM ranking push the total lower still; Airalo sells 1GB Spain-region plans from $4.00, and Firsty's Classic tier starts at €0.98 per GB.

Buying four eSIMs does not mean managing four accounts. Airalo confirms that one account can purchase multiple copies of the same eSIM, with each QR code then scanned on a different phone, and lets you name each eSIM to track which child needs a top-up. GigSky goes further and builds its family offer around this: one primary account manages every plan, labels each device, monitors each member's usage in real time and tops up from a single payment method, with plans from $3.99 across 200+ countries.

Family bundles and multi-device discounts: mostly a myth

We specifically researched whether any mainstream provider sells a true family bundle - a shared pool of data across several eSIMs, or a discount for buying four at once. The short answer is that almost nobody does.

Airalo's own family-travel guidance recommends either individual plans or hotspot sharing; there is no group pricing. Firsty's help centre is explicit that it "does not offer family account plans that consolidate multiple devices under one eSIM". GigSky offers centralised management rather than a price break. The closest things to a family discount we found were indirect: Yesim pays both sides of a referral €5 (so a family of four inviting each other can bank €15 towards plans, though terms limit stacking), plus 5% cashback in its Ycoins currency, and Firsty's free tier effectively makes additional low-priority devices cost nothing. Loyalty credits, such as Saily's 3% back on purchases, accumulate faster when one account buys for four people.

Treat any third-party site advertising an eSIM "family plan" with caution; in every case we checked, it described ordinary individual plans bought several times over.

Kids' devices: check eSIM support before you buy anything

The family eSIM plan collapses if a child's phone cannot host an eSIM. Every iPhone from the XS and XR (2018) onwards supports eSIM, which covers most hand-me-downs still in circulation, but an iPhone 8 or X passed down from a parent is physical-SIM only. Budget and older Android handsets are patchier: many popular kids' models, including cheaper Samsung A-series and Motorola devices from before roughly 2022, shipped without eSIM support even when newer versions have it. Check Settings on the actual handset before the trip - searching for "eSIM" in the settings menu takes ten seconds and avoids an airport surprise.

For a non-compatible device there are three workable options. Buy a local prepaid physical SIM on arrival, which our eSIM vs local SIM guide walks through country by country. Let that child ride on a parent's hotspot, accepting the range and battery limits above. Or leave the phone on Wi-Fi only, which many parents of younger children quietly prefer anyway.

Parental controls: travel eSIMs will not do this for you

None of the mainstream travel eSIM providers offers parental controls or content filtering. We checked the published feature lists and help centres of Airalo, Holafly, Nomad, Saily, Yesim, Firsty and GigSky, and found no blocking, filtering or time-limit features aimed at children. The nearest thing is Saily's security toolkit - an ad blocker and web protection that blocks malicious sites, included free on every plan - which is genuinely useful (an independent audit found the ad blocker cut data consumption by up to 28.6%) but is not a parental control.

The good news is that you do not need the eSIM to do this job. Apple's Screen Time and Google's Family Link operate at the device level, so every restriction you set at home - app limits, content filters, purchase approvals - continues to work identically on a travel eSIM abroad. Set them up before departure, because some changes require both parent and child devices online on a trusted connection. DNS-based filtering apps work the same way over any data connection.

Teenagers and data budgets

Teenagers are the reason the "one big plan" fantasy exists. Short-form video is the killer: at typical quality settings, an hour of TikTok or Instagram Reels consumes roughly 0.8-1GB, so a teen filling two idle hours a day can burn through 15-20GB on a 10-day trip. Our guide to estimating eSIM data needs has detailed per-app figures, but the family-level rule of thumb is simple: parents doing maps, messages and photos need 0.5-1GB a day; teens left unmanaged need 1.5-2GB a day.

That asymmetry is why the mixed setup in our cost table works so well. Give the heaviest user the unlimited plan and everyone else fixed data, or give the teen a Firsty unlimited day pass (from €2 per day, 5GB at full speed then 512 Kbps) only on the days it is needed, since it can be paused. Whatever you choose, check the top-up terms before departure - and check the refund policies too, because an unused or non-functioning child's eSIM is only refundable with some providers. Yesim, notably, advertises a 100% refund guarantee within 30 days.

Keeping the family reachable

Most travel eSIMs are data-only, so the family group chat becomes the switchboard. WhatsApp, Signal and FaceTime all work normally over data, and each person keeps receiving messages to their existing number since the eSIM sits alongside the home SIM in the phone. Warn children not to answer conventional calls or send SMS through the home SIM, as those still incur roaming charges - the same trap covered in our eSIM vs roaming guide.

If you genuinely need a dialable number abroad - for restaurant bookings, theme park callbacks or an older relative who will not use WhatsApp - three options stand out. Airalo's Discover+ global eSIM includes a US (+1) number with calls and texts. Saily offers calling and SMS through an optional phone number add-on. Yesim sells virtual numbers alongside its data plans. Holafly's single-trip plans, by contrast, are data-only and include no number.

Advantages and disadvantages

One shared plan + hotspot

Pros: cheapest possible outlay; one installation; young children need no setup at all. Cons: Holafly's 1GB/day sharing cap makes it unworkable there; shared 2-3GB/day fast-data thresholds elsewhere; battery drain; the family must stay together; single point of failure.

Individual eSIMs

Pros: independence; resilience; data sized to each person; barely more expensive than one premium plan. Cons: more setup (four QR codes); requires every device to be eSIM-compatible and unlocked; top-ups need occasional attention.

Who should choose what

  • Family of four, kids aged 10+, normal budgets: Saily fixed-data plans for everyone, purchased from one parent account.
  • Couple with a toddler or primary-age children: one Airalo unlimited plan on a parent's phone, shared by hotspot when needed. Avoid Holafly for this job.
  • Family with a streaming teenager: mixed setup - unlimited for the teen, fixed data for everyone else.
  • Kids on old non-eSIM handsets: parents on eSIMs, children on a local physical SIM or Wi-Fi only.
  • Parent who wants one dashboard: GigSky, with every device labelled and monitored from one account.
  • Multi-country itineraries: any of the above using regional plans; see our multi-destination ranking.

The verdict

Ranked on price, transparency, hotspot support, plan flexibility and ease of family management, the individual-eSIM strategy wins for most families, and Saily is the best all-round provider to build it on: $51.96 covered our whole test family for ten days, the app is simple enough for a 12-year-old, and the free ad blocker meaningfully stretches every allowance. Airalo takes second place and is the outright winner for anyone committed to sharing one plan, because it is the only major provider that puts "no limit on tethering" in writing; our full Airalo review covers its wider strengths. GigSky earns third for parents who value one-account control over the lowest price.

Holafly remains an excellent single-device product, but its 1GB daily hotspot cap on single-trip plans makes it the wrong choice as a family's shared connection. Firsty deserves a special mention: a free, ad-supported eSIM is a genuinely useful tool for a child's phone that only needs messages and maps. Match the strategy to your family's shape first, then the provider, and the connectivity bill for a 10-day trip should stay comfortably under $60.

Frequently asked questions

Can we install one eSIM on two phones?

No. An eSIM profile locks to the device it is activated on and cannot be duplicated, as providers such as Firsty state explicitly. Each device needs its own eSIM, though one account can buy and manage several.

Do we need a separate account for each family member?

Usually not. Airalo confirms one account can buy multiple eSIMs and scan each QR code on a different phone, and GigSky is built around one parent account managing the whole family. Children never need their own logins.

Is hotspot sharing free on travel eSIMs?

Where it is allowed, sharing itself costs nothing extra; tethered devices simply draw from the plan's data. The limits are the issue: Holafly caps sharing at 1GB per day on single-trip plans, while Airalo and Yesim impose no tethering restriction.

What about a child's phone that does not support eSIM?

iPhones older than the XS/XR (2018) and many budget Androids are physical-SIM only. Buy a cheap local SIM on arrival, share a parent's hotspot, or keep the device on Wi-Fi. Our eSIM vs local SIM guide compares the options.

Do any travel eSIMs include parental controls?

No mainstream provider offers content filtering or screen-time controls. Use Apple Screen Time or Google Family Link, which work at device level on any network; Saily's free ad blocker and web protection add a modest safety layer but are not substitutes.
