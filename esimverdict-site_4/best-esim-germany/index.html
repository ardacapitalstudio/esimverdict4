Best eSIM for Germany in 2026: Ranked Plans, Prices and Network Quality

The best eSIMs for Germany in 2026 compared on price, coverage and network quality, with honest advice on rural dead zones, train signal and registration law.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 13 min read

Germany is one of Europe's easiest countries to stay connected in, right up until it isn't. City centres have fast 5G, the big airports have solid coverage, and any decent travel eSIM will get you online within minutes of landing. Step onto a regional train, drive through the Black Forest or the countryside of Mecklenburg, and you meet the other Germany: the one with famous dead zones that locals call Funklöcher. Which eSIM you buy matters less than most guides suggest for the cities, and more than they admit for everywhere else.

For a short trip our research points to Airalo as the best all-round choice, Saily as the best value for fixed-data plans, and Holafly as the pick for genuinely heavy users who want unlimited data. For longer stays and expats settling in, Ubigi and aloSIM deserve a look. The honest catch that runs through this whole guide is that almost every travel eSIM for Germany rides the Telefónica (O2) or Vodafone network rather than Deutsche Telekom, and in rural Germany that difference is real.

This guide explains what each provider actually gives you, which network it uses, and where the coverage gaps sit, so you can match the plan to your trip rather than the headline price.

German mobile networks: the part that actually decides quality

Germany has three established mobile networks and a fourth still finding its feet.

Deutsche Telekom runs the country's premium network. In the long-running connect and independent test rankings it consistently finishes first for coverage and reliability, and it holds up best in rural areas and along transport routes. If a plan explicitly rides Telekom, that is the quality marker to look for.

Vodafone Germany sits a clear second. Coverage in populated areas is strong and its 5G footprint is wide, though rural performance trails Telekom.

Telefónica (O2) is the third network and by far the most common one that travel eSIMs use, because its wholesale access is the cheapest. O2 has improved enormously since its early reputation for congestion, and in cities it is perfectly good. In the countryside and on trains it is the weakest of the three big networks.

1&1 is Germany's fourth operator, still building its own antenna sites and leaning heavily on national roaming. It is not a network you will knowingly buy a travel eSIM on, and its own coverage remains thin.

Here is the uncomfortable truth for eSIM shoppers. Airalo's Germany plan runs on O2, Ubigi uses Telefónica, Nomad and Holafly use Vodafone and O2, and aloSIM uses a mix of O2, Vodafone and the old E-Plus spectrum. None of the mainstream travel eSIMs we checked guarantees Deutsche Telekom access. In Berlin, Munich or Frankfurt this will not bother you. If your trip involves rural Bavaria, the Harz mountains or long stretches of regional rail, a Telekom-based local plan will simply work in more places, and that is a genuine reason some long-stay visitors still buy locally despite the hassle.

Funklöcher, trains and the coverage reality

Germany's official figures look reassuring. The Bundesnetzagentur's coverage monitor put 4G at roughly 97.8 percent of the country's area from at least one operator as of December 2025, 5G at about 95.3 percent, and mobile broadband of some kind above 98 percent. Those are area figures from a single provider, though, which is exactly where the gap between the statistics and the experience opens up.

The regulator separates "white spots" (no proper mobile broadband, only 2G) from "grey spots" (covered by some operators but not all). If your eSIM rides O2 and you are standing in a grey spot that only Telekom covers, you have no signal even though the area counts as covered. This is why the network your provider uses matters more in Germany than in most of Western Europe.

Trains are the classic problem. Germany's rail lines cut through cuttings, tunnels and rural stretches, and mobile signal on regional and long-distance services is notoriously patchy. The networks and Deutsche Bahn have been closing gaps along the busiest corridors, and O2 in particular has hit rail-coverage expansion targets, but anyone who commutes by train will tell you that a video call from a moving ICE is still a gamble. If reliable connectivity on the move matters to you, an unlimited plan with a strong high-speed allowance beats a cheap capped one, because you will burn data reconnecting. The providers in detail

Airalo: the safe all-rounder

Airalo's German eSIM offers both fixed-data packages and unlimited plans. Unlimited runs from $11 for 3 days up to $59 for 30 days, with the standard fixed-data plans starting around $4.50 for 1GB. It uses the Telefónica O2 network with one additional partner, supports 4G and 5G, and allows hotspot use. The unlimited plans give a daily high-speed allowance before throttling, which resets each day. Airalo is data-only, so calls and texts run over apps like WhatsApp.

The reasons it tops the list are breadth and polish. The app is highly rated, top-ups are simple, pricing is transparent, and support is responsive. It is not the cheapest per gigabyte and it does not ride Telekom, but for most visitors it is the lowest-risk choice. Our full Airalo review goes deeper.

Saily: best value for fixed data

Saily, from the team behind NordVPN, has the cleanest fixed-data pricing we found: 1GB for $4.49, 5GB for $12.99, 10GB for $19.99 and 20GB for $25.99 on 30-day validity, plus an unlimited plan at $48.99 across 10 to 30 days. It supports 5G, allows hotspot use without a stated cap, and its app includes some useful privacy extras. If you know roughly how much data you need and want the best rate per gigabyte, Saily is hard to beat. See our Saily review for the detail.

Holafly: for heavy, unlimited users

Every Holafly Germany plan is unlimited, priced by duration from $11.90 for 3 days to $73.90 for 30 days. It connects to Vodafone and Telefónica O2, supports 5G where available, and applies a fair-use policy that can slow speeds at peak times before resetting daily. Hotspot sharing is capped at 1GB per day, which is the main limitation for anyone wanting to tether a laptop all day. Holafly is the priciest option here and it is data-only, but for streaming-heavy travellers who never want to watch a data counter, it is the most reassuring pick. If you are weighing it against Airalo, our Holafly vs Airalo comparison breaks it down.

Nomad: cheap unlimited and solid fixed data

Nomad splits the difference nicely. Fixed data is competitive (10GB for $15, 20GB for $22, 50GB for $35) and its short unlimited plans are among the cheapest around, from $11 for 3 days to $30 for 10 days. It uses Vodafone and O2, and after the daily high-speed allowance it throttles to around 512kbps rather than cutting you off. Hotspot is supported. It is a strong value choice for a week or two.

aloSIM: the one with a phone number

aloSIM is worth singling out because most of its Germany plans include a phone number with calls and SMS, which is unusual for a travel eSIM. Fixed data runs from $4.50 for 1GB up to $26 for 20GB, with unlimited daily-style plans from $11.50 for 3 days. It uses O2, Vodafone and E-Plus spectrum with a 3GB daily high-speed threshold before dropping to 1 Mbps. If you need to receive an occasional call or text on a normal number rather than only using apps, this is the standout.

Ubigi: built for longer stays

Ubigi, a brand of Transatel and NTT, is aimed more at the frequent traveller than the one-week tourist. In Germany it runs on Telefónica's network, supports 4G and 5G, and allows tethering. Pay-as-you-go plans run from $5 for 500MB to $39 for 10GB, and it also offers monthly and annual subscriptions, including a 20GB monthly plan at $44 and an unlimited monthly plan at $140. For an expat's first months or a repeat business visitor, those recurring plans are more convenient than buying a fresh travel eSIM each trip.

Yesim and the marketplaces

Yesim offers flexible pay-as-you-go and unlimited day passes for Germany from around $2.28 per day, connecting through multiple European operators including Vodafone and Telefónica, with hotspot enabled by default. Marketplace platforms such as MobiMatter list large-allowance plans from resellers like eSIMGo and RoamVault (for example 20GB for around $12.99 or 50GB for $24.99), which can be excellent value if you want a lot of data cheaply, though support quality varies by the underlying seller.

Registration law: why an eSIM is genuinely easier here

Germany has one of Europe's strictest SIM registration regimes. Since July 2017, and now under the Telecommunications Act, every newly activated SIM must be tied to a verified identity and a German address. Buying a cheap prepaid SIM in a supermarket does not get you online at the till; you still have to complete identity verification by Video-Ident, PostIdent at a post office, or in person at an operator's shop. For a visitor with limited German, that is a real barrier.

International travel eSIMs sidestep all of this. They are sold under the provider's own arrangements, so you install, activate and connect without showing ID or proving an address. That convenience is one of the strongest practical arguments for an eSIM in Germany specifically, and it is a big part of why we would steer most short-term visitors away from a local prepaid SIM. We compare the two routes in detail in our eSIM vs local SIM guide.

EU roaming and multi-country trips

Germany sits inside the EU's "roam like at home" zone, so any EU-based plan works across the bloc at no extra charge, and most regional travel eSIMs treat Germany as part of a Europe-wide bundle. If your trip pairs Germany with Austria, the Netherlands, France or beyond, a regional plan usually beats a Germany-only one on both price and convenience. Advantages and disadvantages

Advantages of a Germany travel eSIM

  • No ID verification or German address required, unlike local prepaid SIMs.
  • Instant activation on arrival, with plans from a few dollars.
  • Easy to top up, and easy to swap to a regional plan for wider travel.
  • Strong 5G in cities from Vodafone- and O2-based providers.

Disadvantages to weigh

  • Almost none ride Deutsche Telekom, the strongest rural and rail network.
  • Most are data-only; calls and SMS rely on apps unless you pick aloSIM.
  • Unlimited plans throttle after a daily high-speed allowance.
  • Coverage in Funklöcher and on regional trains can still fail regardless of provider.

Who should choose what

Short city break or business trip: Airalo for reliability and ease, or Saily if you want the best fixed-data price.

Heavy streaming or tethering all day: Holafly for true unlimited, accepting the higher price and the 1GB daily hotspot cap.

Budget-focused week or two: Nomad for cheap unlimited, or a MobiMatter large-data plan if you want maximum gigabytes for the money.

Need calls or texts on a real number: aloSIM.

Expat settling in, or a repeat visitor: Ubigi's monthly plans, used to bridge the gap before you sort a German contract. The wider first-month checklist, including Anmeldung and address registration, is in our eSIM guide for expats.

Rural Germany or serious rail travel: consider a Telekom-based local plan despite the registration hassle, because network choice outweighs provider polish once you leave the cities.

The verdict

For most travellers in 2026, Airalo is the best eSIM for Germany. It balances price, a wide plan range, a strong app and dependable support, and it covers the cities where most visitors spend their time. Saily is our value pick for anyone who prefers to buy a set amount of data, and Holafly is the clear choice for heavy users who want unlimited without watching a counter. Ubigi and aloSIM round out the field for longer stays and for people who need a phone number.

The honest caveat is the one worth repeating: these are convenience-first products riding O2 and Vodafone, not Deutsche Telekom. In Berlin, Munich, Hamburg or Cologne that is a non-issue. If your Germany is the rural and railway one, buy an unlimited plan for the reconnection headroom, keep your expectations realistic about the dead zones, and know that no travel eSIM has fully solved the Funklöcher problem yet.

Frequently asked questions

Which network does the best eSIM for Germany use?

Most travel eSIMs use Telefónica O2 or Vodafone, because their wholesale access is cheaper than Deutsche Telekom's. Airalo and Ubigi ride O2, while Holafly and Nomad use Vodafone and O2. Telekom offers the strongest rural and rail coverage, but few international eSIMs guarantee it, so if you need Telekom quality you may have to buy a local plan.

Do I need to register my identity to use an eSIM in Germany?

No. German law requires ID verification for local prepaid SIMs, but international travel eSIMs are sold under the provider's own terms and need no ID or German address. That is one of the main reasons an eSIM is more convenient than a local SIM for short visits.

Is 5G available on eSIMs in Germany?

Yes. Germany's 5G footprint covers roughly 95 percent of the country's area from at least one operator, and providers such as Airalo, Saily, Holafly and Ubigi support 5G where the underlying network offers it. Speeds in cities are excellent; rural 5G is thinner.

Will my eSIM work on German trains and in the countryside?

Sometimes. Germany has well-known dead zones, and signal on regional and long-distance trains is patchy regardless of provider. An unlimited plan helps because you burn data reconnecting, but no travel eSIM fully fixes the coverage gaps in remote areas.

Can I use one eSIM across Germany and other EU countries?

Yes. Germany is in the EU roaming zone, and most providers sell regional Europe plans that treat Germany as one of many included countries. For multi-country trips a regional plan is usually cheaper and simpler than a Germany-only one.
