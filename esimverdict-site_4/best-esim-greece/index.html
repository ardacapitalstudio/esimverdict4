Greece is an easy country to stay connected in - until you leave Athens. The mainland has strong 4G and growing 5G from all three networks, but Greece is also 200-plus inhabited islands, and once your ferry leaves Piraeus the question stops being "which eSIM is cheapest" and becomes "which network still shows bars in a Cycladic village at 9pm in August". That is the lens for this guide.

Budget travellers should look at Ubigi's $10 for 10GB, and there are genuinely useful options from Yesim, Holiday.com, GigSky and Jetpac too.

Greek networks: Cosmote, Vodafone and Nova

Greece has three mobile networks, and which one your eSIM connects to matters more here than in most European countries.

Cosmote is the largest operator, owned by OTE (itself majority-controlled by Deutsche Telekom). It has the strongest reputation for rural and island coverage, and it is consistently the network Greeks themselves recommend for remote areas. The catch for eSIM buyers: none of the travel eSIM providers we researched openly advertises Cosmote as its Greek partner. If you want Cosmote, the realistic route is a local prepaid SIM (covered below).

Vodafone Greece is a solid second choice. It was among the first to switch on 5G, having spent around 130 million euros on spectrum in December 2020 and launched 5G service in January 2021. Holafly and Nomad both list Vodafone among their Greek networks.

Nova is the former Wind Hellas, rebranded in January 2023 after United Group bought the operator for roughly 1 billion euros and merged it with Nova Greece. Historically the third network by subscribers, it has invested heavily since the merger. Airalo lists Nova as its primary Greek network, and Nomad's "Wind" partner is the same infrastructure.

In Athens, Thessaloniki and the big resort towns you will barely notice the difference. Gaps show up in small island villages, mountain interiors and at sea, where Cosmote tends to hold signal longest and Nova has traditionally been patchiest. A dual-network eSIM such as Nomad's hedges this risk, which is a large part of why it tops our ranking.

Island coverage: the real differentiator

Mainland Greece is straightforward. Expect fast data in cities, reliable 4G along motorways, and 5G in urban centres. The islands are where planning pays off.

Cyclades (Santorini, Mykonos, Naxos, Paros). Main towns and ports are well covered by all networks. Coverage thins on remote beaches, hiking paths between villages, and the quieter western islands. Santorini's caldera-edge villages generally perform well; the island draws an estimated 2 million visitors a year, so capacity, not coverage, is the issue there (more below).

Dodecanese (Rhodes, Kos, Symi, Karpathos). Rhodes and Kos towns are strong. Smaller islands rely on fewer masts, so a village can drop from 4G to bare-minimum signal within a short walk. If your itinerary leans on the smaller Dodecanese, a dual-network eSIM or a Cosmote prepaid SIM is the safer bet.

Ferries between islands. Greek island ferries mostly hug coastlines, so your eSIM will usually cling to shore-based signal, with dead patches mid-crossing. Be careful on larger vessels: some ships operate satellite-based maritime networks, which sit outside both EU roaming rules and travel eSIM coverage and can be very expensive if your phone latches on. Turn off data roaming for any network name you do not recognise at sea. Our guide to eSIMs on cruises covers maritime networks and how to avoid accidental charges in detail.

Summer congestion: Santorini and Mykonos in August

Coverage maps do not show congestion. In July and August, Santorini and Mykonos concentrate enormous numbers of tourists - plus cruise calls that add thousands of phones to a small town for a few hours - onto island cell sites never designed for that density. Customer reports across providers describe the same pattern: full signal bars but crawling speeds at Oia sunset viewpoints, ports at ship turnaround times, and beach clubs in peak season.

You cannot buy your way around a saturated mast, but a dual-network eSIM lets your phone shift to whichever network is less loaded, and plans with real high-speed allowances hurt less than throttled "unlimited" tiers when the mast is already struggling. Download maps, tickets and boarding passes before the evening peak. Our guide to eSIM reliability explains why congestion, not coverage, causes most travel connectivity complaints.

The rankings

Our ranking weighs eight factors: price, coverage, network quality, plan flexibility, transparency, ease of use, hotspot support and customer service. For Greece specifically we weight island network access and value per usable gigabyte most heavily.

1. Nomad - best overall for Greece

Nomad's Greece line-up is the strongest we found. Fixed plans run from 1GB for $4 up to an aggressive 50GB for 30 days at $31 - by far the cheapest per-gigabyte rate among the big names - with 10GB at $18 and 20GB at $21 in between. Unlimited plans start at $11 for 3 days. Crucially, Nomad states its Greece eSIM uses both Wind (Nova) and Vodafone with automatic network switching, which is exactly the hedge you want for island travel. Plans must be activated within 60 days of purchase.

The caveats: on unlimited plans, once you exhaust the daily high-speed allocation you are throttled to 512 kbps until the daily reset, which is slower than some rivals' floors. Support is app and chat based. For most Greece trips, the 10GB or 20GB fixed plans are the sweet spot.

2. Saily - easiest all-rounder

Saily (from the team behind NordVPN) keeps things simple: 1GB for $4.49, 3GB for $9.99, 5GB for $13.99, 10GB for $22.99 and 20GB for $35.99, with unlimited listed at $48.99 across duration options from 5 to 30 days. Hotspot use is unrestricted, activation is automatic on arrival, and 24/7 live chat support is genuinely responsive by reputation. The app also bundles security extras such as ad blocking.

Saily does not disclose which Greek network it uses, which costs it transparency points, and its per-gigabyte pricing sits above Nomad's. It remains the pick if you want the least possible friction. Read our full Saily review for details.

3. Airalo - best for multi-country trips

Airalo's Greece page now leads with unlimited plans, from $11.50 for 3 days to $72 for 30 days, riding primarily on Nova. The more interesting product for many travellers is regional: Airalo's EU+UK eSIM starts at $4.00 and covers Greece alongside the rest of Europe, which makes it the natural choice if Greece is one stop on a longer route - say Athens after a week in Italy. For itineraries spanning several countries, see our multi-destination eSIM guide.

Airalo's scale (200+ destinations) and mature app are strengths; its single-network Greece footprint and middling customer-review scores are the weaknesses. Our Airalo review goes deeper.

4. Holafly - best unlimited

Holafly sells only unlimited plans for Greece: $11.90 for 3 days, $27.50 for 7, $73.90 for 30. Unlike most "unlimited" products there is no fixed daily high-speed cap; Holafly's fair-use policy instead allows temporary throttling for "no more than one day" in rare congestion cases. It uses Vodafone and Nova in Greece, supports 5G where available, and includes roughly 1GB per day of hotspot data (a 7-day plan carries 7GB of shareable data). Every purchase now also includes 1GB of backup data monthly across 150+ countries.

It is data-only, there is no top-up on single-trip plans, and it is the most expensive option here per day. Heavy streamers and remote workers who cannot budget data are the target market. See how it compares in Holafly vs Airalo.

5. Ubigi - best cheap single plan

Ubigi, the eSIM brand of NTT-owned Transatel, tops the independent esimdb popularity table for Greece with a straightforward 10GB, 7-day plan at $10.00. That is $1 per gigabyte from an established operator-backed provider, and it suits the classic one-week island holiday almost perfectly. Ubigi's catalogue and app are less polished than the travel-first brands, and its Greek network partner is not clearly disclosed, but on pure value for a single trip it is hard to argue with.

6. Holiday.com - best for repeat visitors

Holiday.com (built by the ExpressVPN team, though no VPN is included or required) covers Greece within its 150+ destinations. European plans start from $3.50, and its unlimited tier gives 3GB per day at full speed, then unlimited data at 10 Mbps - one of the more generous throttle floors around. Its structural advantage is the single re-toppable eSIM: install once, top up for every future trip, with up to a year to activate a purchased plan. Greece-specific pricing sits in its purchase portal rather than on public pages, a transparency niggle we would like to see fixed.

7. Yesim - best for short stays and stopovers

Yesim's pay-as-you-go structure starts around $0.51 (€0.45) for a 500MB day pass in Greece, which is ideal for a cruise stop or a weekend where a 30-day bundle makes no sense. The app supports flexible top-ups and extras such as virtual numbers. Per-gigabyte costs climb quickly if you scale usage, so it is a short-stay tool rather than a two-week solution. If you have an EU SIM: check roaming first

If your home SIM was issued in the EU or EEA, you may not need a travel eSIM at all. "Roam like at home" rules let you use your domestic minutes, texts and data in Greece at no extra charge, subject to fair-use limits, and the framework remains in force with wholesale data caps falling to €1 per GB by 2027. Heavy data users on capped "roam like at home" allowances may still find a cheap local eSIM useful as a top-up. UK SIM holders lost this protection after Brexit and should check their operator's Greece policy - several reintroduced roaming charges. Our eSIM vs roaming guide walks through the maths.

The local prepaid alternative

A local Greek prepaid SIM is the only easy way to get onto Cosmote's network, which matters if your trip centres on smaller islands or remote areas. Tourist-oriented prepaid packages from Cosmote (What's Up), Vodafone and Nova are sold in operator shops and electronics chains in every sizeable town, typically with generous data for 10 to 20 euros, plus a Greek number for calls.

The friction is registration: Greek law requires identity registration for prepaid SIMs, so you will need your passport and a few minutes in a shop - kiosk-counter anonymity is not an option. For stays over a month, or for travellers who need maximum island coverage, the trade-off is worth it. For a one-week holiday, a travel eSIM installed before departure wins on convenience.

Advantages and disadvantages of using an eSIM in Greece

Advantages

  • Installed and working before you land in Athens - no airport queues or shop visits
  • Fixed-data plans from $4, far below non-EU roaming rates
  • Dual-network options (Nomad, Holafly) hedge island coverage gaps
  • Keeps your home SIM active for calls and banking SMS
  • Regional plans cover Greece plus the rest of Europe on one eSIM

Disadvantages

  • No travel eSIM we found openly offers Cosmote, the strongest island network
  • Nearly all plans are data-only - no Greek number for restaurant bookings or ferry-line calls
  • "Unlimited" plans carry daily caps or fair-use throttles in most cases
  • August congestion on Santorini and Mykonos affects every network and every eSIM
  • EU/EEA residents may already roam free, making an eSIM redundant

Who should choose what

  • One-to-two-week island holiday: Nomad 10GB or 20GB, or Ubigi's 10GB for $10 if a week is enough
  • Simplicity above all: Saily - install, land, connect, with 24/7 chat if anything breaks
  • Multi-country European trip: Airalo EU+UK regional from $4.00
  • Remote work and heavy streaming: Holafly unlimited, or Holiday.com if you want a 10 Mbps floor rather than a fair-use policy
  • Cruise or brief stopover: Yesim day passes, or GigSky's free 500MB starter
  • A month or more, or small remote islands: local Cosmote prepaid SIM, passport in hand

The verdict

Nomad is our best eSIM for Greece in 2026. It combines the lowest cost per gigabyte of any major provider ($31 for 50GB), stated access to two of the three Greek networks, and unlimited options for those who want them - the complete package for a country where network redundancy genuinely matters. Saily takes the runner-up spot for sheer ease of use, and Airalo's regional plan is the right call the moment Greece becomes one stop among several. Pay Holafly's premium only if you truly cannot predict your usage, grab Ubigi's $10 plan for a cheap week in the sun, and remember: if your SIM is from an EU country, check your roaming terms before spending anything at all.

Frequently asked questions

Which mobile network is best in Greece?

Cosmote, the largest operator, has the strongest reputation for coverage, particularly on islands and in rural areas. Vodafone Greece is a close second with 5G live since January 2021, and Nova (the former Wind) has improved since its 2023 merger under United Group. In cities the difference is minor; on small islands it can be significant.

Do eSIMs work on the Greek islands?

Yes. Popular islands such as Santorini, Mykonos, Rhodes, Corfu and Crete are well covered by all three networks that travel eSIMs use. Expect weaker signal on remote beaches, in mountain villages and mid-crossing on ferries. Dual-network eSIMs such as Nomad's give you the best chance of holding a usable connection.

Is 5G available in Greece for tourists?

Yes, in cities and larger resort areas. Vodafone Greece launched 5G in January 2021 and the other networks have followed. Most travel eSIMs, including Nomad, Holafly, Saily and GigSky's free starter plan, state 5G access where the local network provides it. On smaller islands you will mostly see 4G, which is fine for maps and streaming.

Can I make calls with a Greece eSIM?

Almost all travel eSIMs for Greece are data-only, so there is no Greek phone number. Calls over WhatsApp, Viber (widely used in Greece), FaceTime or Signal work normally on data. If you need a real local number - for restaurant reservations or Greek ferry helplines - buy a registered local prepaid SIM instead.

How much data do I need for a week in Greece?

Around 5GB covers maps, messaging, social media and light photo uploads for most travellers. Streaming, video calls and hotspot use push that towards 10 to 15GB, which is why Ubigi's 10GB week for $10 and Nomad's 10GB for $18 hit the sweet spot.
