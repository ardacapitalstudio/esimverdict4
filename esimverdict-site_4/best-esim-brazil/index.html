Best eSIM for Brazil in 2026: Prices, Networks and CPF-Free Data Compared

We compare Brazil eSIM prices, networks and unlimited plans from Nomad, Airalo, Saily, Holafly and more - no CPF needed. Ranked for 2026 travellers.

Brazil is one of the few big destinations where a travel eSIM is not merely convenient but close to essential. Foreigners buying a local prepaid SIM are routinely asked for a CPF, the Brazilian tax number, and although the rules were officially relaxed years ago, shop staff still turn away passport-holding tourists often enough that relying on a local SIM is a gamble. A travel eSIM installed before you fly sidesteps the whole problem.

Our research for this guide checked current Brazil pricing from seven providers on 19 July 2026. The short answer: Nomad is our overall pick, riding the Vivo network with 10GB for $22 and a sensibly priced unlimited option, while Airalo is the best choice for travellers heading beyond the big cities because its Brazil eSIM uses TIM, the network with the country's widest 4G footprint. Budget travellers can get 1GB for $3.99 from Saily, and heavy users should weigh Ubigi's 10GB for $17 against Holafly's unlimited-but-pricey daily plans.

Because Brazil runs on WhatsApp, a data-only eSIM costs you almost nothing in practice. Hotels, restaurants, tour operators and taxi drivers all message rather than call, so the lack of a local number matters far less here than almost anywhere else.

How Brazil's three networks compare

Every travel eSIM in Brazil ultimately connects to Vivo, Claro or TIM, so the network behind your provider matters more than the brand on the app.

Vivo (Telefónica) has the strongest overall signal in the country and is the operator independent guides most often recommend for rural areas. TIM has the widest raw coverage, with roughly 98.9% of the population reached by its 4G network, historically built out along Brazil's highways. Claro typically posts the fastest LTE speeds but with a smaller footprint than the other two. All three run 5G in the state capitals and major cities, and 5G coverage thins out quickly beyond them.

One pattern is worth internalising: coverage in Brazil tracks wealth and population density. São Paulo, Rio, Brasília and the coastal capitals are well served by all three networks, often on 5G. Small interior towns may have a single usable network, and the gaps between towns can be total dead zones. The CPF problem: why travel eSIMs win in Brazil

Brazil registers every SIM to an identity, and the identity Brazilian systems expect is the CPF tax number. The government relaxed the rules back in 2012 to let operators accept passport details instead, and TIM does allow passport registration. In practice, though, compliance is patchy: independent reporting found Vivo shops in Rio de Janeiro refusing to sell SIMs without a CPF as recently as January 2024, Claro requires a CPF for its regular prepaid products, and many operator apps and even public Wi-Fi portals demand a CPF at login.

You can obtain a CPF as a foreigner, including through Brazilian consulates before travel, but for a two-week holiday it is paperwork nobody needs. Vivo and Claro now sell tourist eSIMs that skip the CPF requirement, which is a tacit admission of the problem. A travel eSIM bought from any provider in this guide asks for none of it: pay by card, scan a QR code, land connected. The seven providers in detail

1. Nomad - best overall (65/80)

Nomad's Brazil eSIM runs on Vivo, the network with the country's best overall signal, and its pricing is competitive at every tier: 1GB/7 days for $4.50, 5GB/30 days for $13, 10GB/30 days for $22 and 20GB/30 days for $35. Its unlimited plans, $17 for 5 days or $31 for 10, carry a daily high-speed allowance (2GB/day under Nomad's standard policy) before throttling to 512kbps until the next 24-hour cycle. Tethering is supported, and add-on top-ups extend a trip without reinstalling. Watch the 60-day activation window: buy too early and the clock can beat your flight.

2. Airalo - best beyond the cities (63/80)

Airalo names its Brazilian partner openly: TIM, whose 4G reaches more of Brazil's population than any rival. That makes Airalo our pick for itineraries through the interior, the Northeast backlands or long bus routes. Data-only plans run from 1GB/3 days at $4.00 to 20GB/30 days at $36. Unusually, Airalo also sells voice-inclusive Brazil plans (for example 3GB, 30 minutes and 30 SMS for $10 on 30 days) for travellers who genuinely need to ring ahead. There is no unlimited option for Brazil, and Airalo's Trustpilot score sits around 3.9, the weakest of the majors. Our full Airalo review covers the wider service.

3. Saily - budget entry, unrestricted hotspot (61/80)

Saily undercuts everyone on the entry tier at $3.99 for 1GB/7 days, and its 30-day mid-tier plans ($9.99/3GB, $13.99/5GB, $24.99/10GB) suit longer stays. Hotspot use is explicitly unrestricted, which matters for couples sharing one plan. The unlimited option is expensive, listed from $48.99, and Saily does not publish which Brazilian network it uses, which costs it transparency points. Plans auto-activate 30 days after purchase, so do not buy months ahead.

4. Ubigi - heavy-data value (58/80)

Ubigi's 10GB/7 days for $17 is the best large-bundle price we verified, and it is one of the few providers connecting to two Brazilian networks, TIM and Vivo, so your phone can fall back if one is weak. The catch is fit: 7 days is short for 10GB unless you are a genuinely heavy user, and Ubigi's app and setup are more utilitarian than Airalo's or Saily's. Its world plans (1GB/30 days at $16) are poor value for a Brazil-only trip.

5. Holafly - unlimited convenience at a price (56/80)

Holafly sells only unlimited data for Brazil, on Vivo, at $12.50 for 3 days rising to $95.90 for 30, roughly $3.20 to $4.20 per day. There is no hard data cap, but Holafly's fair-use policy allows temporary speed reductions during network congestion, and hotspot sharing is capped at 1GB per day, which two map-hungry companions will drain fast. Its Trustpilot score of about 4.6 and 24/7 chat support are genuine strengths. If your two weeks in Brazil would need more than about 3GB a day, Holafly beats buying 40GB of fixed data; otherwise Nomad or Ubigi is cheaper. See Holafly vs Airalo for the fuller head-to-head.

6. MobiMatter - marketplace bargains (54/80)

MobiMatter's marketplace lists Brazil deals from multiple wholesale providers, from $3.99 for a LATAM 1GB/7-day plan and $4.49 for Brazil-specific 1GB. Its regional LATAM listings are worth a look for travellers combining Brazil with Argentina or Peru, a use case our multi-destination eSIM guide covers in depth. The trade-off is variability: support quality, refund rules and the underlying network differ by listing, so read each product page carefully.

7. Yesim - pay-as-you-go for light users (50/80)

Yesim's verified Brazil entry price is a 200MB day pass at $0.52 for new users, and its Pay & Fly wallet bills per megabyte from a balance valid 12 months, which suits travellers who barely use data. We rank it last on transparency grounds: it does not disclose its Brazilian network partner, and its throttling thresholds are poorly documented. Our full Yesim review explains the caveats.

Coverage warnings: the Amazon and the interior

No eSIM fixes physics. In the Amazon region, mobile coverage is effectively an urban service: Manaus, Belém, Santarém and the larger river towns have usable 4G, but expect nothing between settlements, on multi-day boat journeys or inside forest lodges, many of which rely on satellite links or generator-powered Wi-Fi. The same applies, less severely, to the Pantanal, Chapada national parks and long stretches of interior highway.

Practical mitigations: download offline maps and translation packs before leaving a city; if connectivity is critical, favour TIM-based plans (Airalo) or dual-network Ubigi for breadth, or Vivo-based plans (Nomad, Holafly) for rural signal strength; and treat any lodge's "Wi-Fi available" claim as aspirational. Estimate your needs honestly with our guide to how much eSIM data you need - streaming habits that are trivial in São Paulo will burn through a plan quickly if you cache content for offline days.

WhatsApp makes data-only painless

Every plan in this guide except Airalo's voice bundles is data-only, and in Brazil that is barely a limitation. WhatsApp is the country's default channel for everything: restaurant bookings, tour confirmations, apartment check-ins, even customer service from banks and airlines. Calls to Brazilian businesses happen over WhatsApp voice, which runs happily on any of these plans. The one genuine gap is receiving SMS verification codes from your home bank, which is a reason to keep your home SIM active on roaming for receiving texts while your eSIM carries the data.

Advantages and disadvantages of a Brazil travel eSIM

Advantages

  • No CPF, no shop queue, no Portuguese-language registration battle.
  • Installed and tested before you fly; connected on landing.
  • Prices from $3.99, far below most operators' roaming rates - see eSIM vs roaming.
  • WhatsApp voice and video replace a local number for nearly all purposes.

Disadvantages

  • Data-only in almost every case: no Brazilian number for SMS codes.
  • You inherit one network's dead zones; locals can swap SIMs, you mostly cannot.
  • Unlimited plans carry daily speed caps or congestion clauses.
  • Local prepaid data is cheaper per gigabyte if you can actually buy it.

Who should choose what

  • City break in Rio or São Paulo: Nomad's 5GB or 10GB on Vivo, or Saily if you want the cheapest start and free tethering.
  • Touring the interior, Northeast or long bus routes: Airalo on TIM for the coverage footprint.
  • Amazon or Pantanal trips: Ubigi's dual TIM+Vivo access for breadth, plus offline maps regardless.
  • Heavy users and content uploaders: Ubigi 10GB/$17, or Holafly unlimited if you genuinely exceed 3GB a day.
  • Two weeks, zero thought, money no object: Holafly, accepting the hotspot cap.
  • Multi-country South America: MobiMatter's LATAM listings or a regional plan from our multi-destination guide.
  • A few megabytes a day: Yesim's wallet, eyes open.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best eSIM for Brazil in 2026?

Nomad is our overall pick: it uses the strong Vivo network, sells 10GB/30 days for $22 and offers unlimited plans from $17 for 5 days. Airalo is the better choice for travel outside the major cities because its Brazil plans ride TIM, the network with the widest 4G coverage in the country.

Do I need a CPF number to use an eSIM in Brazil?

No. Travel eSIMs from Nomad, Airalo, Saily, Holafly and the rest require no Brazilian documentation at all. The CPF problem only affects local SIMs: despite rules allowing passport registration, shops (particularly Vivo and Claro) frequently still demand a CPF from foreigners, which is precisely why travel eSIMs are so attractive in Brazil.

Which Brazilian network is best: Vivo, Claro or TIM?

It depends where you go. Vivo has the strongest overall signal and is most recommended for rural areas, TIM has the widest 4G population coverage at roughly 98.9%, and Claro typically records the fastest LTE speeds in cities. All three offer 5G in state capitals. In São Paulo or Rio any of them is fine; in the interior the differences show.

Will my eSIM work in the Amazon?

Only in towns. Manaus, Belém and the larger river settlements have usable 4G on the major networks, but there is no coverage between settlements, on river journeys or at most jungle lodges. Download offline maps before leaving a city and treat lodge Wi-Fi as a bonus, not a plan.

How much data do I need for a trip to Brazil?

Most travellers using maps, WhatsApp, social media and ride-hailing get through 300-500MB a day, so 5GB covers a typical two-week trip with margin. Heavy video calling or uploading pushes that towards 1GB+ a day, where Ubigi's 10GB or an unlimited plan makes more sense. Our data calculator guide breaks down usage by app.
