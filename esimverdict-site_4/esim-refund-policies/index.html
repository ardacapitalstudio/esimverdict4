Travel eSIM Refund Policies Compared (2026): Airalo, Holafly, Saily and More

We compared the published refund policies of nine travel eSIM providers - refund windows, used-plan rules, credit-only traps, and what to do when a refund is refused.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 16 min read

Travel eSIM refund policies range from genuinely generous to effectively non-existent, and the difference is buried in terms and conditions most travellers never read. At one extreme, Holafly will refund an unused eSIM up to 6 months after purchase. At the other, GigSky's terms state flatly that a plan "once sold cannot be returned" and that unused balance "will not be refunded". Most providers sit somewhere in between, with one near-universal rule: the moment you activate the eSIM and consume data, your easy refund disappears.

The details matter more than the headlines. Airalo pays change-of-mind refunds in Airmoney store credit rather than cash. Yesim gives you just 1 hour to report an app malfunction for an out-of-window refund. MobiMatter, as a marketplace, defers parts of its policy to whichever of its 30+ operators sold you the plan. And every provider handles the commonest complaint - "it did not work at my destination" - by demanding evidence that you troubleshot while still there.

This guide compares the published refund policies of nine providers, verified against their terms and help centres on 19 July 2026, then sets out a practical playbook: what to check before buying, what evidence to keep, and the escalation path when a refund is refused.

At a glance

  • Most generous unused-plan policy: Holafly - full refund up to 6 months after purchase if the eSIM was never activated
  • Best structured policy: Saily - 30 days, with a clear rule that activated plans still qualify if more than 99% of the data remains
  • Cash-vs-credit trap: Airalo pays "wrong purchase" and change-of-mind refunds only in Airmoney store credit; Yesim converts refunds of 10 EUR or less into Ycoins credit
  • Strictest policies: GigSky (no refunds on pay-as-you-go plans, discretionary otherwise) and Ubigi (nothing back once the QR code is scanned)
  • Marketplace complication: MobiMatter refunds depend partly on the underlying operator's own cancellation rules and fees
  • Universal rule: activation is the cliff edge - under EU and UK law your 14-day cooling-off right can lawfully end the moment supply of digital content begins with your consent
  • Chargebacks: a card-scheme remedy of last resort, typically within 120 days, but not a legal right - exhaust the provider's process first

Where refunds are won and lost

Activation is the cliff edge

Every policy above pivots on the same event. Before installation and activation you are, in most cases, holding a returnable product. Afterwards you hold consumed digital content, and the provider's obligations shrink to fixing genuine faults. Saily's terms express the industry norm most clearly: a plan not installed, or installed but not activated, gets a full refund, while an activated plan qualifies only if "more than 99% of the Data Plan balance" remains. Ubigi draws the line even earlier: scan the QR code and "your refund request will be denied".

The practical consequence: never install an eSIM you might not use, and resolve any compatibility doubt before the QR code, not after. A related trap: deleting an eSIM profile usually destroys it, and providers treat customer-side deletion as your problem - MobiMatter's exclusions say so explicitly. Our guide to recovering a deleted eSIM explains why prevention is the only reliable cure.

Credit is not cash

Airalo's policy offers refunds "either as Airmoney, which is an in-app reward currency, or credit back via your original payment method" - but the choice is not always yours. Its help centre reserves the original-payment route for installation and technical failures; wrong purchases and changes of plan are refunded in Airmoney only. For a frequent customer that is a minor inconvenience. For someone who tried the service once, it is a refusal wearing a refund's clothes. Our Airalo review covers how Airmoney interacts with the loyalty scheme.

Yesim applies a smaller version of the same practice: refunds of 10 EUR or less are paid in Ycoins, the app's non-refundable internal credit. Given how many single-country plans cost under 10 EUR, small purchases effectively refund into the app, not your bank account.

The marketplace complication

MobiMatter's policy has to serve a store selling plans from more than 30 operators, and it shows. Malfunction refunds run through MobiMatter's own 24-hour troubleshooting rule, but cancelling an unactivated plan carries "cancellation / return fees... in line with the relevant operator policies" - fees that differ by operator and are not shown at checkout. Processing can take 10 to 45 days. None of this makes MobiMatter a bad buy; its prices are frequently the market's best. It does mean the refund experience is less predictable than with a vertically integrated provider, a trade-off we examine in Holafly vs MobiMatter.

"It didn't work at my destination"

This is the claim providers see most, and every policy handles it the same way: evidence demands and a time limit. Holafly requires you to contact support during your trip, warning that post-trip reports cannot guarantee refunds, and asks for screenshots to verify device incompatibility or locking. MobiMatter requires you to run its troubleshooting tool and give support 24 hours before any refund is considered. Airalo makes refunds conditional on your cooperation and gives itself 10 days to restore access. Ubigi accepts claims only for faults proven to originate with its parent network, Transatel.

Support teams typically ask for screenshots of your APN settings, data-roaming toggle, selected network and error messages, because most "no service" complaints turn out to be configuration issues or local congestion rather than a dead eSIM. The traveller who keeps screenshots and chat transcripts is in a far stronger position than the one who quietly buys a local SIM and asks for a refund a fortnight later. Our eSIM reliability guide explains how to tell provider failures from network failures.

Your legal backstop: EU and UK consumer law

If you buy online from within the EU, you normally have 14 days to cancel a distance purchase for any reason. But the cooling-off right has a specific exception for "online digital content, if you have started downloading or streaming it". The UK kept the same structure: under regulation 37 of the Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013, the right to cancel digital content ends once supply has begun, provided you gave express consent and acknowledged losing the right - the small print you agree to at checkout, and the reason Airalo can lawfully end its 14-day withdrawal right at activation.

Two useful corollaries survive. First, an unactivated eSIM is not yet supplied digital content, so within 14 days of purchase an EU or UK consumer has a strong legal claim to a full cash refund regardless of the provider's policy - store-credit-only terms sit on shaky ground here. Second, a UK trader who failed to obtain proper consent and acknowledgement cannot charge for the content at all. Quoting the exact regulation in a complaint email has a way of changing its trajectory.

Chargebacks: the option of last resort

When a provider refuses and you believe the refusal is wrong, your card offers a final route. Chargeback is a Visa, Mastercard and Amex scheme process, not a legal right, that lets your bank reverse a transaction for services not provided or not as described, typically within 120 days. It works on debit and credit cards with no minimum spend, which matters because the UK's stronger Section 75 protection only covers credit card purchases over £100 - more than almost any eSIM costs.

Be realistic about the odds. The provider will answer the dispute with its usage logs, and if those show your eSIM connected and consumed data, "service not provided" becomes hard to sustain. Chargebacks succeed most often for unactivated eSIMs, duplicate charges and merchants who stopped replying. File one only after the provider's own process has failed, attaching your receipt, the policy text, screenshots and dated support transcripts.

What Trustpilot complaints reveal

Refund friction shows up clearly in review patterns our research tracked through July 2026. Airalo's Trustpilot score sits around 3.9-4.0 across roughly 29,000 reviews, with recurring complaints about slow, scripted support on technical claims and refunds arriving as Airmoney when reviewers expected cash. Holafly scores about 4.6-4.7 from over 100,000 reviews, with strong marks for the 6-month unused window but friction on post-activation claims, which its policy leaves to case-by-case analysis. MobiMatter's 4.3 from over 47,000 reviews mixes praise for pricing with frustration about escalations stalling between marketplace and operator. The pattern: unused-plan refunds mostly happen as promised, while "it did not work" claims succeed or fail on the evidence the traveller kept.

Advantages and disadvantages of the leading policies

Generous policies (Holafly, Saily)

  • Advantages: long windows (6 months unused at Holafly, 30 days at Saily); clear thresholds; cash refunds; predictable processing.
  • Disadvantages: Holafly's post-activation outcomes are discretionary and can carry a $3.50 fee; Saily's 99%-unused rule means one accidental connection can void eligibility.

Middle ground (Airalo, Yesim, Nomad)

  • Advantages: real refund routes for technical faults; Airalo's 14-day withdrawal mirrors your statutory right; Yesim's 30-day window is fair on paper.
  • Disadvantages: credit-not-cash practices at Airalo and Yesim; Nomad's eligibility varies plan by plan with no published processing times; Yesim's 1-hour reporting window for app malfunctions is the tightest we found.

Strict policies (MobiMatter, Ubigi, GigSky)

  • Advantages: MobiMatter's 24-hour malfunction rule is at least concrete; Ubigi processes approved refunds within 10 business days.
  • Disadvantages: operator-dependent fees and 10-45 day processing at MobiMatter; nothing back after a QR scan at Ubigi; explicit no-refund terms at GigSky.

The practical playbook

Before you buy

  1. Check device compatibility yourself. Dial *#06# and look for an EID; an unlocked, eSIM-capable phone is your responsibility, and Ubigi excludes locked devices from refunds entirely.
  2. Check real coverage, not the country list. A provider "covering" a country says nothing about which local network you will ride on - the distinction our eSIM reliability guide is built around.
  3. Screenshot the refund clause before checkout. Policies change; your screenshot fixes the version you bought under. While in the small print, check the fee traps in our guide to hidden eSIM costs.
  4. Do not activate early, and do not delete. Leave activation for arrival, and never remove the profile while you might still claim.
  5. Pay by card, not app-store balance, so the chargeback route stays open.

The escalation path when refused

  1. Reopen the ticket with evidence: dates, screenshots of settings and errors, and the exact policy clause covering your case. Ask for the reason for refusal in writing.
  2. Cite your statutory rights if you are in the EU or UK and the eSIM was never activated within 14 days of purchase - name the Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013 or the EU Consumer Rights Directive and request a cash refund.
  3. Escalate internally: ask for a supervisor review or use the provider's formal complaints address (Nomad publishes hello-cs@getnomad.app).
  4. Post a factual Trustpilot review. Large providers respond publicly within days, and documented refund cases are frequently resolved after one - keep it factual, not furious.
  5. File a chargeback within your card scheme's window, usually 120 days, with the full evidence trail attached.
  6. For EU purchases, the European Consumer Centres network and the provider's local consumer authority remain free further steps.

Who should choose what

  • You plan trips far ahead, or plans change often: Holafly. The 6-month unused window is unmatched.
  • You want the fairest written rules: Saily. Clear thresholds, defined decision times, cash refunds.
  • You are a repeat traveller in one ecosystem: Airalo's Airmoney-first practice barely hurts, since credit gets spent on the next trip anyway.
  • You chase the lowest price and accept the risk: MobiMatter - but treat every activated purchase as final, and run its troubleshooting tool the moment anything misbehaves.
  • You want no refund anxiety at all: buy late, activate on arrival, and favour long activation windows such as Holiday.com's 1-year runway, which removes the commonest reason refunds get requested.

The verdict

Judged on refund windows, clarity, payout method and processing speed, Holafly and Saily publish the strongest policies in the travel eSIM market, and GigSky and Ubigi the weakest. Airalo lands mid-table: its terms look consumer-friendly, but the Airmoney practice means occasional users should treat purchases as final. MobiMatter's marketplace prices come with the least predictable refund path, a trade-off worth making only with eyes open.

The larger truth is that policy quality matters less than traveller behaviour. Almost every refused refund in the complaint patterns we reviewed shared one of three causes: the plan was activated before the problem was found, the problem was reported after the trip, or no evidence was kept. Buy late, keep screenshots, report from the destination, and even a middling policy will usually pay out. Skip those steps and even Holafly's 6 months will not save you.

Frequently asked questions

Can I get a refund if my eSIM never worked at my destination?

Usually yes, but only if you contacted support while still at the destination and cooperated with troubleshooting. Holafly warns that post-trip reports cannot guarantee refunds, and MobiMatter requires a troubleshooting request and a 24-hour fix window. Keep screenshots of your APN settings, network selection and error messages as evidence.

Which eSIM provider has the most generous refund policy?

Holafly, for unused eSIMs: a full refund up to 6 months after purchase, provided the eSIM was never activated, paid to your original payment method in 5-10 business days. For activated plans, Saily's rules are the most generous we found, refunding non-subscription plans with over 99% of data remaining inside 30 days.

Do I lose my 14-day cooling-off right when I activate an eSIM?

In the EU and UK, generally yes. The law allows the cancellation right for digital content to end once supply begins with your express consent, which you give at checkout. Before activation, though, the 14-day right stands, and it entitles you to a cash refund rather than store credit.

Can I do a chargeback if an eSIM provider refuses a refund?

Yes, on Visa, Mastercard and Amex debit or credit cards, normally within 120 days of the transaction. It is a card-scheme process rather than a legal right, and providers can contest it with usage logs, so it works best for unactivated eSIMs, duplicate charges or unresponsive merchants. Exhaust the provider's own process first.

Will I get my money back in cash or store credit?

It depends on the provider and the reason. Holafly, Saily, MobiMatter and Ubigi refund to your original payment method. Airalo pays change-of-mind refunds in Airmoney credit, reserving cash for technical failures. Yesim refunds to your original method except amounts of 10 EUR or less, which become Ycoins app credit.
