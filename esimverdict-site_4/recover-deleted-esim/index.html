Can You Recover an eSIM After Deleting It by Mistake?

Deleted your eSIM by accident? Recovery depends entirely on the provider. We compare Airalo, Holafly, Nomad, Saily, Ubigi and more, plus what to do first.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 14 min read

Sometimes yes, sometimes no - and the difference is almost entirely down to which provider sold you the eSIM. Saily states you can reinstall a deleted eSIM and even move it to a different device, Ubigi allows up to five reinstallations through its app, and Holafly lets you reinstall multiple times on one device at a time. At the other end of the scale, Airalo warns that most of its eSIMs cannot be reinstalled at all, and Nomad's help centre notes that QR codes "often expire after first use".

The reason deletion feels so final is architectural. An eSIM is not an app you can re-download from a store; it is an operator profile provisioned onto a chip in your phone under the GSMA's remote SIM provisioning specification. Delete the profile and it is gone from the device - whether you can get a working replacement depends on what the provider's server is willing to issue, not on anything your phone can do.

This guide compares eight providers' documented reinstallation and replacement policies, explains what support teams can and cannot regenerate, and sets out the steps to take in the first half hour after an accidental deletion, when your odds of a free fix are highest.

At a glance

  • Best odds of recovery: Saily (reinstall on the same or a different device via your account), Ubigi (up to 5 reinstalls via the app) and Holafly (multiple reinstalls, one device at a time).
  • Worst odds: Airalo (most eSIMs cannot be reinstalled) and Nomad (most plans cannot move devices; QR codes often expire after first use).
  • Same device only: Holiday.com - a deleted eSIM "can only be reinstalled on the same device".
  • It depends: MobiMatter, where the answer follows the underlying operator's rules, and "deleting a one-time-use QR code could permanently disable the eSIM".
  • Golden rule: deleting an eSIM is never a troubleshooting step. Disable it instead - a disabled profile can be re-enabled in seconds.

Why deletion is usually permanent at the profile level

Consumer eSIMs run on the GSMA's remote SIM provisioning specification. When you scan a QR code, your phone contacts the provider's provisioning server, which releases an operator profile for download onto the phone's embedded SIM chip. The QR code is not the eSIM; it is a claim ticket, and most travel providers configure that ticket to be honoured once.

Nomad states it plainly: "eSIMs cannot typically be transferred to another phone. QR codes often expire after first use, and export/import options are limited." MobiMatter is blunter still, warning that "deleting a one-time-use QR code could permanently disable the eSIM". Once your phone erases the profile, scanning the same code again typically returns an error, because the server has already marked it as used.

The one-shot design has a purpose - it stops one purchased plan being cloned across several phones - but the burden lands on whoever taps "Delete eSIM" at the wrong moment. Some providers engineer around it by tying the eSIM to your account rather than to a single code, which is why Saily and Ubigi can offer app-based reinstallation while others cannot.

Removing a plan from the device is not removing it from the account

Deleting the profile in your phone's settings does not delete your purchase. Your order, its remaining data balance and its expiry date all live on the provider's systems, and support can still see them. What has gone is the device-side credential that connects you to the network.

This cuts both ways. A provider can sometimes re-issue the profile against the same order, exactly as Ubigi and Saily do by design. But the validity clock keeps running while you negotiate - a 7-day plan deleted on day 2 is still expiring in the background, which is why speed matters.

What support can and cannot regenerate

Our research across eight help centres found a consistent pattern. Support teams can usually resend your original QR and manual activation codes by email, as Holafly's team does; check whether your specific plan permits reinstallation, as Airalo's app does automatically; and, on account-based systems, release the profile for a fresh download. Where the underlying operator allows it, some can generate a genuine replacement QR - MobiMatter notes that operator policies range from direct transfer through "replacement QR code generation" to strict single-device limits.

What support cannot do is resurrect the deleted profile itself. If the operator treats each profile as single-issue, no amount of chat escalation changes that; the remedies are a goodwill replacement or a refund claim. Nor can anyone move a device-locked eSIM onto different hardware: Airalo states that if the original phone is broken or lost, "the eSIM and any remaining data cannot be recovered or transferred".

The first 30 minutes: an escalation checklist

  1. Stop. Do not buy a replacement yet. A panic repurchase can undermine any later refund conversation, and several providers can restore you for free.
  2. Try the in-app route first. Airalo's app can check whether your plan supports reinstallation; Ubigi's app has a "Re-install my Ubigi eSIM" option under Settings; Saily reinstalls from the app on the same account.
  3. Gather your evidence. Order number, purchase email, account email address, device model. Keep the original QR email if you have it - Holafly's recovery route is re-scanning those same codes.
  4. Open live chat and be specific. Say the profile was deleted from the device by mistake, that the device is unchanged, and ask whether the profile can be re-released or a replacement code issued against the same order.
  5. Stay on the same phone. Several policies (Holiday.com, Holafly, Nomad's Amber exception) only work on the original device or after a clean delete-then-install sequence.
  6. Note the clock. If the plan is actively expiring, say so in the first message and ask for the validity to be adjusted if the fix takes more than a few hours.

Prevention: the habits that make deletion a non-event

Label every eSIM the moment you install it. A profile named "Business Trip" or "MobiMatter Turkey Oct" does not get deleted by someone tidying a settings screen; "Travel 3" does. Nomad publishes a help article specifically on renaming eSIMs, and iOS and Android both allow custom labels.

Never clean up mid-trip. Phones hold plenty of profiles - Apple says an iPhone stores eight or more eSIMs - so there is no need to delete anything while abroad. Disable unused lines instead; a disabled eSIM costs nothing and revives instantly, as our guide to running two eSIMs at once explains. Delete old travel eSIMs at home, after the trip, once any refund window has closed.

Treat deletion as the last resort in troubleshooting, never the first. Most connection problems are roaming toggles, APN settings or network selection - all covered in our eSIM reliability guide. MobiMatter's help centre states outright that "deleting an eSIM should not be used as a troubleshooting step".

Plan for phone upgrades. Apple's eSIM Quick Transfer moves a SIM to a new iPhone only "if your carrier supports eSIM Quick Transfer" - and travel eSIM providers generally are not among the supporting carriers, so assume a travel profile will not survive the move. Android transfer tooling is newer and patchier still, varying by manufacturer and carrier. The safe sequence: run down travel plans before upgrading, or choose a provider such as Saily whose account model survives a device change. Corporate handsets add another layer - on managed iPhones, IT can block eSIM changes entirely, as our corporate devices guide covers.

When recovery fails: where refunds fit in

Accidental deletion sits in an awkward spot in most refund policies, because the service worked and the mistake was yours. Airalo pays refunds to the original payment method only for installation or technical failures; a "wrong purchase" claim is settled in Airmoney store credit. Holafly's 6-month refund window applies to eSIMs that have not been activated or used - little help for a plan deleted mid-trip, though its liberal reinstallation policy means you rarely reach that point. MobiMatter allows refunds only for "genuine product malfunction" unresolved within 24 hours, with outcomes governed partly by the underlying operator's policies.

If the profile cannot be re-issued, your realistic outcomes are a goodwill credit, a partial refund at the provider's discretion, or a write-off. It costs nothing to ask, and support agents resolve accidental-deletion cases daily. The full picture of who refunds what, and how to frame a claim, is in our eSIM refund policies guide; polite, specific, well-documented requests recover money surprisingly often.

Advantages and disadvantages of today's recovery landscape

What works in your favour

  • Account-based providers (Saily, Ubigi, Yesim) have made deletion largely reversible - the eSIM belongs to your login, not to a single QR code.
  • Your purchase and data balance survive on the provider's side even when the device profile is destroyed.
  • Support teams can resend original codes, check plan-level reinstallation rights and sometimes issue replacement QRs.
  • 24/7 chat at Holafly, Saily and Holiday.com means the time-critical first hour is covered in any timezone.

What works against you

  • One-time activation codes remain the industry default; Airalo and most Nomad plans treat deletion as final.
  • The plan's validity clock keeps running while you seek help.
  • Device-locked profiles (Holiday.com, Airalo) cannot follow you to a new or replacement phone.
  • User error is excluded from most refund policies, so a failed recovery often means paying twice.

Who should choose what

  • You delete things by reflex, or share the phone with someone who does: choose an account-based provider. Saily has the clearest policy - reinstall on the same or a different device with the same account email - and Ubigi's five-reinstall allowance is nearly as forgiving.
  • You switch phones often: Saily again, or accept that with Airalo, Nomad, Holafly and Holiday.com a mid-plan device change means losing the plan. Our Airalo review covers its one-device rule in detail.
  • You want one eSIM that never needs reinstalling: Holiday.com's model - a single re-toppable eSIM per device with a 1-year activation window - or Yesim's single reusable profile removes the delete-reinstall cycle for multi-destination travellers, as long as you keep the profile installed.
  • You buy through MobiMatter: check the specific listing's operator terms before you travel, and run the troubleshooting wizard before touching the delete button.

The verdict

A deleted eSIM is recoverable more often than the industry's warnings suggest - but only if you bought from the right provider and act quickly on the right device. Saily, Ubigi and Holafly have effectively solved accidental deletion; Airalo and Nomad mostly have not, and their one-time codes make a moment's misclick an unrecoverable loss on many plans. Until account-based reinstallation becomes standard, the practical policy is simple: label your profiles, disable rather than delete, keep every activation email, and if the worst happens, open the provider's app and then its chat within minutes, not days. Recovery is a race between your support ticket and your plan's expiry clock.

Frequently asked questions

Can support just send me a new QR code?

Sometimes. Holafly will resend your original QR and manual codes by email, and its eSIMs support repeated installation on one device at a time. On operator-locked products - most Airalo packages, many MobiMatter listings - the original code is spent once used, and a fresh QR requires the operator to issue a replacement profile, which is discretionary and not guaranteed.

Does deleting the eSIM from my phone cancel my data plan?

No. Deletion removes the credential from the device; the plan, its remaining data and its expiry date continue to exist on the provider's account systems. That is precisely why recovery is sometimes possible - and why the validity clock keeps ticking while you arrange it.

Will my travel eSIM transfer automatically when I get a new iPhone?

Assume not. Apple's eSIM Quick Transfer works only where the carrier supports it, and travel eSIM providers generally do not. Airalo states its eSIMs cannot be transferred to another device at all, and Holiday.com's can be reinstalled only on the original phone. Saily is the notable exception, allowing reinstallation on a different device through your account.

I deleted my eSIM mid-trip - is my remaining data gone?

On Saily, Ubigi, Holafly and Yesim, usually not: reinstall via the app or your saved codes and the same plan resumes. On Airalo, the app will check whether your specific plan can be recovered; if it cannot, the remaining data is lost and support may offer credit. On Nomad, unless your eSIM was supplied by its partner Amber, expect to repurchase.

How do I remove an eSIM safely once a trip is over?

Wait until you are home and the refund window has passed. Check the plan has fully expired in the provider's app first, since some providers let expired eSIMs be re-topped for future trips - Holiday.com is built around exactly that. Then delete only the profile you have positively identified by its label.
