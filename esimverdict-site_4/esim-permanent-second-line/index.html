Can You Use a Travel eSIM as Your Permanent Second Line?

Most travel eSIMs expire within 30 days, but a few genuinely work as a permanent second line. We compare Honest Mobile, Airalo Discover+, Holafly and more.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 15 min read

Mostly, no - and the reasons are baked into the product. A typical travel eSIM is a prepaid data bundle with a validity clock of 7 to 30 days, no phone number, and terms written for a fortnight's holiday rather than a five-year relationship. Let the plan lapse and the data vanishes; delete the profile and, with many providers, it cannot even be reinstalled.

But a small group of products now genuinely work long-term. Honest Mobile sells a purpose-built backup eSIM for £3.75 a month. Airalo's Discover+ global eSIM bundles a +1 phone number with packages that run a full year. Holafly has moved into rolling monthly subscriptions, Yesim rents standalone virtual numbers from $3.60 a month, light plans from giffgaff and US Mobile cost less than many assume, and Umi's pay-as-you-go Flow balance claims never to expire at all.

This guide explains why the mainstream model breaks down, which products solve the problem, what each costs over a year, and the small print that decides whether your second line is still alive when you finally need it.

At a glance

  • Standard travel eSIMs fail as permanent lines on three counts: validity of days not years, no phone number (or a virtual one), and credit that quietly expires.
  • Cheapest genuine backup: Honest Mobile's Smart SIM at £3.75/month billed annually - app-restricted unlimited data across EE, O2, Three and Vodafone, roaming in 180+ countries, no number.
  • Cheapest line with a number: giffgaff from £5/month, or pay as you go kept alive with one chargeable action every six months.
  • Best global option with a number: Airalo Discover+ - a +1 number with calls and texts, up to 20GB/365 days for $89.
  • Set-and-forget data with no standing cost: Umi's Flow balance (claimed no expiry) or Airalo's data-only Discover 20GB/365 days at $66.
  • The catch list is real: dormancy clauses, eKYC passport checks, VoIP numbers some banks refuse, and terms that lag the marketing.

Why most travel eSIMs fail as a permanent line

Validity expiry is built into the product

Travel eSIMs are priced as short-term roaming bundles. Airalo's cheapest headline plans now run just 3 days, most fixed bundles top out at 30, and even "long" plans rarely pass 90. When the clock runs out, unused data is gone - and because most profiles are single-use, a deleted eSIM often cannot be reinstalled. Nomad plans must even be activated within 60 days of purchase. A product that dies of natural causes within a month is not a permanent line, merely a disposable one you keep re-buying.

No number, or a number banks distrust

The overwhelming majority of travel eSIMs are data-only: fine for WhatsApp, useless for anything that insists on ringing or texting you. Where numbers exist, they are usually virtual - Airalo's Discover+ provides a US-based +1 number, Holafly's subscription number handles inbound SMS and VoIP calling only, and Yesim's numbers are app-based. These work for many sign-ups, but customer reports suggest some banks and government services reject virtual and VoIP ranges for SMS verification, and none of these providers publishes a route to port the number away. A number you cannot port and cannot guarantee your bank will accept is a convenience, not an identity.

Credit expiry and dormancy clauses

Even money loaded into these ecosystems has a shelf life. Umi's published terms give vouchers 180 days of validity, and Airalo's change-of-mind refunds arrive as Airmoney store credit rather than cash. Cheap SIMs have their own trap: giffgaff's terms disconnect any SIM unused for six months, at which point "you will lose any remaining credit or Plan balance" and "we will not be able to reconnect once a number is disconnected and recycled". A second line is only permanent if you or your billing keep it demonstrably alive.

The products that genuinely work long-term

Honest Mobile Smart SIM - the purpose-built backup (UK)

Honest Mobile's Smart SIM was designed for exactly this job. For £10 a month, or £3.75 a month billed annually at £45, you get a data-only eSIM with unlimited use of more than 500 "essential" apps - WhatsApp, Google Maps, Uber, Monzo, ChatGPT and similar - that connects to EE, O2, Three and Vodafone in the UK and kicks in automatically when your main SIM completely loses signal. Roaming is included in 180+ countries, with a 14-day money-back guarantee.

The limits matter: no phone number, no calls or SMS, no social media or streaming, and no VPN support at present. It is a safety net for the apps that get you home, not a second phone line. For a full line, Honest's main plans start at £12.15 a month with unlimited minutes and texts, a portable UK number and 5G on Three - we weigh the whole operation in our Honest Mobile review.

Airalo Discover+ - a global +1 number on one eSIM

Discover+ is Airalo's global eSIM with calls and texts attached to a US-based +1 number that works internationally. Packages run from 1GB/7 days at $9.00 up to 20GB/365 days at $89, so a single purchase keeps the line technically alive for a full year. Incoming texts are free, minutes round up, and top-ups come as combined data, call and SMS bundles.

Two cautions. First, Airalo publishes no number-retention promise: nothing in its help pages states what happens to the +1 number once every package has expired, so treat continuous validity as essential. Second, the voice service is aimed at travellers, not at anchoring your identity - no porting route is offered. Full context in our Airalo review.

Holafly subscriptions - always-on unlimited with a bundled number

Holafly's monthly plans are the closest a travel brand comes to a proper rolling contract. The Unlimited subscription costs €59.95 a month (15% off paid yearly) for unlimited data in 160+ destinations, hotspot sharing, a phone number for the US, UK or Canada handling inbound SMS and VoIP calls, and a 1GB "Always On" backup allowance that renews every 30 days even between trips. A Light tier at €45.95 a month carries 25GB but no number, and both cancel any time. Superb for someone permanently on the move; hard to justify as a dormant standby at over €600 a year.

Yesim virtual numbers - a number without a plan

Yesim, a Swiss super-app we cover in our eSIM super-apps guide, sells virtual numbers separately from data: from $3.60 a month for numbers in the US, UK, Canada, France, the Netherlands or Israel, receiving SMS and handling calls through the app, active for as long as the subscription runs. Yesim markets them for verifications on Google, WhatsApp, Telegram, PayPal and similar, but some services detect and refuse VoIP ranges, so test critical accounts early. Pair one with any cheap data plan and you have a modular second line for under $50 a year - see our Yesim review for the rest of the app.

giffgaff and US Mobile - real light plans with real numbers

Sometimes the honest answer is: use a real operator's cheap plan instead. In the UK, giffgaff runs on O2 with no contract, bundles from £5 a month, and pay-as-you-go that costs nothing to hold - provided you make one chargeable call, text, data connection or top-up every six months, because clause 13.5 of its terms disconnects unused SIMs. In the US, US Mobile's Unlimited Flex costs $17.50 a month billed annually ($210 a year, taxes included) for unlimited talk, text and data with a 10GB high-speed allowance. These are proper mobile numbers issued by real networks: portable, accepted by banks, usable for years. The trade-off is that they are domestic products, so roaming needs configuring or paying for.

Umi Flow - no-expiry pay as you go data

Umi attacks the expiry problem directly: one reusable "Universal eSIM", a credit balance called Flow that the company says never expires, and per-gigabyte rates from about $2.45 in Europe across 200+ claimed destinations. As a zero-standing-cost standby data line, the idea is exactly right. The caveats: Umi's operator was incorporated only in September 2024, its public review base is a handful of app-store ratings, and its published terms do not yet document the no-expiry promise - a gap we examine in our Umi eSIM review. A promising experiment, not yet a foundation.

Four sensible use cases

Backup data when your main network fails. The strongest case, and the cheapest: Honest's auto-switching Smart SIM, a loaded Umi balance or a 365-day Discover pack all cover it. Redundancy is the theme of our eSIM reliability guide - a second line you never test is not a backup.

A separate number for selling, dating or business. A Yesim number at $3.60 a month keeps Marketplace buyers, dating matches and clients away from your personal number, and can be dropped without ceremony. Just do not attach anything you would mind losing.

A travel-ready standby line. Hold Umi Flow credit or a long-validity Discover pack and there is working data the moment the plane door opens. It runs happily alongside your main SIM - our guide to running two eSIMs at once covers the setup.

Banking and 2FA abroad. Here real numbers win. Keep your home SIM alive for SMS codes, or hold a cheap giffgaff or US Mobile line in the relevant country. Virtual numbers fail unpredictably for banks - a problem that bites hardest in your first weeks overseas, as our guide to the expat's first month explains.

Number retention and porting rules

The dividing line is simple. Numbers from real operators - giffgaff, US Mobile, Honest Mobile's main plans - sit in standard mobile ranges, are accepted almost everywhere, and are portable: in the UK a PAC code moves your number between networks. Retention rules still apply; giffgaff's terms are explicit that recycled numbers are unrecoverable.

Numbers from travel eSIM products - Airalo's +1, Holafly's subscription number, Yesim's virtual numbers - are service features, not owned assets. None of the three publishes any porting route, and retention depends on keeping the underlying subscription active; our research found no provider promising what happens to a lapsed number. Treat them as disposable aliases: excellent for separation, wrong for anything you must keep for years.

The catch list

  • Dormancy clauses. giffgaff disconnects SIMs unused for six months and recycles the number; other prepaid operators have similar rules. Set a repeating calendar reminder.
  • Credit expiry. Umi's terms give vouchers 180 days; Airalo change-of-mind refunds come as store credit.
  • eKYC identity checks. Some Airalo local eSIMs require passport verification that can take days - fine for a planned purchase, hopeless in an emergency.
  • VoIP rejection. Some banks and verification systems refuse virtual number ranges. Test every critical account while you still have an alternative.
  • App-restricted data. Honest's Smart SIM covers 500+ approved apps only - no open browsing, and no VPN support currently.
  • Auto-renewal. Holafly subscriptions activate immediately and roll every 30 days; cancel in the app, not by deleting the eSIM.
  • Terms that lag the product. Umi's contract does not yet describe the Flow balance its homepage sells. With young providers, the written terms - not the landing page - are what you can enforce.

Advantages and disadvantages

Advantages

  • A permanent second line now costs from £45 a year, or £0 standing with Umi Flow or giffgaff PAYG.
  • Multi-network backups connect to whichever mast works, something no single contract offers.
  • Virtual numbers cleanly separate selling, dating and business contacts from your real identity.
  • Long-validity global packs mean instant data in 150+ countries with nothing to buy on arrival.

Disadvantages

  • No travel eSIM brand offers a portable, bank-grade phone number; only real operators do.
  • Standing costs are easy to forget: an unused Holafly subscription burns €600 a year.
  • Dormancy clauses, credit expiry and single-use profiles all punish neglect - the default state of a second line.
  • The most innovative product (Umi) has the least track record; the most capable (Holafly) costs the most.

Who should choose what

  • UK residents who want a safety net: Honest Mobile's Smart SIM at £45 a year, with the auto-switch across four networks doing the thinking for you.
  • Anyone needing a second number for casual use: Yesim's $3.60/month virtual numbers, accepting they cannot be ported and may fail bank checks.
  • Frequent international travellers: Airalo Discover+ 20GB/365 days, or the cheaper data-only Discover pack plus your home SIM for codes.
  • Expats and anyone dealing with banks: a real light plan - giffgaff from £5, US Mobile Flex at $210 a year - kept alive alongside a travel eSIM for data.
  • Experimenters happy to be early: a small Umi Flow top-up as a standby, with an established fallback installed next to it.

The verdict

A standard travel eSIM cannot be your permanent second line; no amount of topping up changes its short-validity, no-number, disposable design. But the market has quietly split. For backup data, purpose-built products now do the job properly and cheaply - Honest Mobile's £3.75-a-month Smart SIM is the most elegant solution we have researched, and Umi's no-expiry model points at where the industry is heading, even if its paperwork has not caught up. For a second number, travel brands offer usable but unportable aliases, with Airalo's Discover+ the most complete. For anything that touches your bank or your long-term identity, the verdict is old-fashioned: rent a real number from a real operator for £5 a month or less, keep it demonstrably alive, and let travel eSIMs do what they are actually good at - cheap, flexible data, everywhere else.

Frequently asked questions

Can a travel eSIM replace my second phone contract?

For data, yes - a long-validity pack or no-expiry balance covers occasional use far cheaper than a contract. For calls, texts and anything requiring a trusted number, no: travel eSIM numbers are virtual, non-portable and not universally accepted.

Does the Honest Mobile Smart SIM include a phone number?

No. It is data-only, restricted to 500+ approved essential apps, with no calls or SMS - those stay on your main SIM. £3.75 a month billed annually buys automatic backup data across EE, O2, Three and Vodafone in the UK plus roaming in 180+ countries.

Will banks accept an Airalo, Holafly or Yesim number for verification?

Sometimes, but do not rely on it. These numbers come from virtual and VoIP ranges, and customer reports suggest some banks and official services reject them for SMS codes. Plenty of sign-ups work fine; for banking 2FA a real mobile number is the safe choice.

What happens to my number if I let the plan lapse?

Real operators publish the rules: giffgaff disconnects SIMs unused for six months and recycled numbers cannot be recovered. Travel eSIM brands publish no retention promise at all, so assume a lapsed Discover+ or subscription number is gone.

What is the cheapest way to keep a permanent second line?

For data only: giffgaff pay as you go (free to hold with one chargeable action every six months), a Umi Flow balance with no standing cost, or Honest's Smart SIM at £45 a year. For a real number with a bundle, giffgaff's £5 plan is the cheapest we verified.
