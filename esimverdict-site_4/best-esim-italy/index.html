Best eSIM for Italy in 2026: Seven Providers Compared and Ranked

We compare Italy eSIM prices, unlimited plans and TIM, Vodafone, WindTre and Iliad network access across seven providers to find the best eSIM for Italy in 2026.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 19 July 2026 13 min read

Italy is one of the easiest countries in Europe to cover with a travel eSIM, and one of the most competitive. Our research for July 2026 found working Italy plans from under $1 for a day of light use to $73.90 for a month of unlimited data, with at least four different Italian networks in play depending on which provider you choose. For most visitors, Nomad currently offers the strongest overall package: fixed bundles from $4, an unlimited option, tethering included and access to two Italian networks.

The provider name on the checkout page matters less than the network behind it. An Italy eSIM connects you to TIM, Vodafone Italy, WindTre or Iliad, and the gap between those networks shows up precisely where tourists go: thick-walled historic centres, high-speed trains, the islands and the crowded piazzas of Rome, Venice and Florence in season. This guide covers who rides which network, what "unlimited" really means on each plan, and when a local SIM or your home operator's roaming makes more sense.

Italy's four mobile networks, and who rides them

Italy has four network operators. TIM (Telecom Italia) is the incumbent with the deepest rural footprint and a long record near the top of independent drive tests. Vodafone Italy is its traditional rival for network quality, particularly strong in cities and along transport corridors. WindTre, formed from the 2020 Wind and Tre merger, runs the largest combined site count and competes on population coverage rather than headline speed. Iliad is the newcomer, building aggressively since 2018; it claims 99 per cent 4G population coverage and 5G in 7,000 municipalities, but traveller reports still place it behind TIM and Vodafone once you leave built-up areas.

The north-south picture matters more than operators admit. All four networks are dense in Milan, Rome, Florence, Bologna and along the A1 corridor. Coverage thins in the rural south - inland Calabria, Basilicata, the Apennine villages - and in Alpine valleys, where TIM and Vodafone generally hold signal longest according to customer reports. Sicily and Sardinia are well covered on the coasts and in Palermo, Catania and Cagliari, with weaker service in the mountainous interiors.

Which network you get depends on your provider. Our checks found Airalo's Italy eSIM connects primarily via WindTre plus two unnamed partners; Holafly uses Vodafone Italy and WindTre; Nomad uses Iliad and Wind; and marketplace seller ESIM.DOG advertises TIM and Wind with 5G. Saily, Yesim and Ubigi do not publish their Italian partners on their plan pages, which costs them transparency points in our ranking. If your itinerary is city-based, any of these will do. If you are driving rural Puglia or hiking the Dolomites, a Vodafone-based or TIM-based plan is the safer bet - see our guide to eSIM reliability for why network partners matter more than brand names.

The best Italy eSIMs in 2026, ranked

1. Nomad - best overall

Nomad's Italy line-up is the most complete we found: fixed bundles from $4 for 1GB to a genuinely cheap $35 for 50GB over 30 days, plus unlimited plans from $11 for three days. Unlimited plans carry a daily high-speed allowance (2GB per day on current terms) before throttling to 512 kbps. Tethering is supported, and some plans include a local number. The catch is the network: Iliad and Wind are the value end of the Italian market, fine in cities but the weakest pairing for remote areas.

Pros: cheapest big bundles; unlimited option; hotspot; transparent throttle terms. Cons: rides Iliad/Wind rather than TIM or Vodafone; unlimited daily cap is modest.

2. Saily - best cheap small plans

Saily, from the team behind NordVPN, sells Italy plans at $3.99 for 1GB up to $28.99 for 20GB, with automatic activation on arrival, unrestricted hotspot use and 24/7 chat. Its unlimited plan is unusual: $48.99 buys a selectable 5-15 day window with a generous 5GB per day at full speed before dropping to 1 Mbps. App ratings around 4.7 and extras such as an ad blocker and virtual location make it the most polished experience here, though it does not name its Italian network partners. Our full Saily review has more detail.

Pros: low entry price; 5GB/day unlimited cap is the most generous listed; strong app and support. Cons: network partners undisclosed; unlimited only worthwhile for heavy users.

3. Yesim - cheapest unlimited

Swiss-based Yesim undercuts everyone on unlimited data: $3.60 per day for short stays, falling to an effective $1.88 per day on a 30-day plan - roughly a quarter of Holafly's price. Fixed bundles are equally sharp, from $2.76 for 1GB to about $1.01 per GB at the 30GB size, and hotspot is supported. New users can trial 500MB for $0.54. Like Saily, it does not disclose which Italian networks it uses, and its fair-use terms deserve a read before you rely on it for a month. See our Yesim review for the wider picture.

Pros: lowest unlimited pricing in this guide; flexible top-ups; hotspot included. Cons: undisclosed network partners; brand less established than the big five.

4. Holafly - most worry-free unlimited

Holafly sells only unlimited plans for Italy: $11.90 for three days, $27.50 for a week, $73.90 for a month. Unlike most rivals it publishes no daily high-speed cap, only a fair-use policy that may briefly slow speeds at peak times and resets daily. It rides Vodafone Italy and WindTre, the strongest network pairing we verified, and offers refunds for up to six months in defined cases. The compromises: it is data-only, hotspot is limited to sharing 1GB per day, and per-day pricing is the highest here. Our Holafly vs Airalo comparison weighs it against the obvious alternative.

Pros: no published daily speed cap; Vodafone-grade coverage; long refund window. Cons: expensive; 1GB/day hotspot limit; no fixed-data cheap option for light users.

5. Airalo - the known quantity

Airalo has moved its Italy country plans to unlimited-only: $11 for three days to $65 for 30 days, on WindTre plus two unnamed partners, with a fair-use structure of 3GB per day at full speed then 1 Mbps. That makes it pricier than Nomad or Yesim for equivalent cover, though its EU and UK regional plan starting at $4 remains a solid budget route that includes Italy. The platform itself is mature, with well-rated apps and a huge user base. Read our full Airalo review before deciding.

Pros: established provider; unlimited across all durations; cheap regional fallback. Cons: no small fixed Italy bundles any more; 3GB/day cap trails Saily; partner networks not fully named.

6. Ubigi - for frequent visitors

Ubigi, owned by NTT, suits people who return to Italy often rather than one-trip tourists. Its reusable eSIM profile stays on your phone for life, with one-off plans (1GB/30 days at $16 on its World tier) and subscriptions such as 5GB per month for $17, up to unlimited at $140 per month, all with tethering and up to 5G. Casual visitors will find single-trip pricing uncompetitive against everyone above.

Pros: keep one eSIM forever; subscriptions for regulars; 5G support. Cons: poor value for a single holiday; Italian network partner not stated on plan pages.

7. Marketplace micro-plans - for day-trippers only

Aggregator listings on esimdb show Italy plans from sellers such as AlpineSIM ($0.42 for 500MB/day), Voyasim and ESIM.DOG ($0.60 for 500MB on TIM/Wind 5G). These can be unbeatable for a day trip or a backup line, but support quality varies widely and refund policies depend on the individual seller. Treat them as spare tyres, not the main wheel - and see our guide to hidden costs in cheap eSIMs before buying on price alone.

Coverage notes for real Italian travel

Historic centres. Renaissance palazzi and medieval stone walls a metre thick are effective signal blockers. Inside museums, churches and ground-floor trattorie in Florence, Siena or Matera, expect bars to drop regardless of provider; 5G's higher frequencies suffer most, and connections often fall back to 4G indoors.

Trains. Frecciarossa onboard wifi draws consistently poor reviews from passengers, so your own data plan does the real work on the Milan-Rome run. The catch is that the high-speed line tunnels repeatedly through the Apennines between Bologna and Florence, where every network drops out; TIM and Vodafone recover fastest along rail corridors according to customer reports.

Islands. Sicily and Sardinia are covered by all four operators, with reliable service in cities and along the coasts. Inland and mountain areas (Madonie, Gennargentu) see thinner coverage, and small offshore islands can serve congested cells in August.

The Vatican. Vatican City has no consumer mobile network of its own; Italian operators blanket St Peter's Square and the museums. A few providers list Vatican City as a separate destination, so an Italy-only plan almost always keeps working as you cross the square, but check the coverage list if you want certainty.

Peak-season congestion. Rome, Venice and Florence in July and August put enormous load on cells around the main sights. Speeds sag in the afternoon crush around the Trevi Fountain or Rialto, and unlimited plans with fair-use clauses (Holafly's included) are the first to be deprioritised. A plan with 5G access clears congested 4G cells noticeably better.

EU roaming and the local SIM alternative

If you already hold a SIM from an EU or EEA country, roam-like-at-home rules mean Italy usually costs nothing extra, subject to your operator's fair-use volume - a travel eSIM only wins on big data needs. Visitors from the UK, US, Australia and elsewhere face daily roaming fees of $5-12 with most home operators, which an eSIM undercuts within a day or two; our eSIM vs roaming guide runs the numbers.

Buying an Italian prepaid SIM in a TIM or WindTre shop remains the cheapest route to a large allowance with calls included, but Italian law requires identity registration: the shop needs your passport and will generate a codice fiscale (Italian tax code) for the contract. It works, but it costs you queue time on day one and is impractical for short stays - the trade-offs are covered in our eSIM vs local SIM comparison.

For multi-country itineraries - Italy plus France, Switzerland or a ferry to Greece - a regional plan beats stacking country eSIMs. Start with our Europe road-trip eSIM guide or our multi-destination eSIM ranking, and if the Adriatic crossing is on your list, our Greece guide covers the other side.

Who should choose what

  • Typical one or two week holiday: Nomad's 10GB or 20GB bundle covers maps, photos and video calls for under $25.
  • Light user or day trips: Saily's $3.99 1GB plan, or a marketplace micro-plan as a throwaway.
  • Heavy streamer or remote worker: Yesim unlimited for value, or Saily unlimited for the biggest full-speed daily allowance.
  • No-stress traveller who never wants to watch a meter: Holafly, for its capless unlimited on Vodafone and WindTre.
  • Rural driving, islands and small villages: favour Vodafone or TIM access - Holafly, or ESIM.DOG's TIM-based plans.
  • Several trips a year: Ubigi's reusable profile and monthly subscriptions.
  • Grand Tour across borders: a regional plan from our multi-destination ranking rather than an Italy-only eSIM.

The verdict

Nomad takes first place for Italy in 2026 on sheer value and flexibility: $4 entry pricing, a $35 50GB bundle and unlimited options, with clearly published throttle terms. Its Iliad/Wind network base is the one genuine weakness, which is why Holafly - dearer, but riding Vodafone Italy and WindTre with no published daily cap - remains our pick for coverage-sensitive itineraries, and Saily and Yesim squeeze the budget end hard. Airalo's shift to unlimited-only Italy plans leaves it competitive but no longer the default choice. Whatever you buy, match the underlying network to your itinerary before you match the price to your wallet.

Frequently asked questions

Does an Italy eSIM work in Vatican City and San Marino?

Vatican City is covered by the Italian networks, so Italy plans work there in practice. San Marino is a separate country on coverage lists; most regional European plans include it, but check an Italy-only plan's list before relying on it.

Which Italian network is best for tourists?

TIM and Vodafone Italy lead most independent assessments for coverage and consistency, especially outside cities. WindTre offers broad population coverage, and Iliad claims 99 per cent 4G coverage but performs best in urban areas. In city-centre Italy the differences are small.

Are unlimited Italy eSIMs really unlimited?

Mostly no. Airalo allows 3GB per day at full speed, Nomad about 2GB and Saily 5GB before throttling. Holafly publishes no daily cap but reserves the right to slow speeds temporarily at peak times. Read the fair-use policy, not the headline.

Can I make calls with these eSIMs?

The plans in this guide are data-only, so ordinary Italian phone calls and SMS are out; WhatsApp, FaceTime and other app calls work normally. Some Nomad plans include a local number, and an Italian prepaid SIM is the better route if you need real calls.

When should I install and activate my Italy eSIM?

Install at home on wifi before you fly, but do not activate until arrival unless the provider states activation starts on first connection. Most plans have installation windows of 30-60 days, and automatic-activation providers such as Saily start the clock when you first connect in Italy.
