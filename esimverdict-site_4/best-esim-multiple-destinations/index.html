Best eSIM for Multiple Destinations in 2026: Regional and Global Plans Compared

Comparing the best eSIMs for multiple destinations in 2026. Airalo, Nomad, Holafly, MobiMatter and Holiday.com regional and global plans, priced and ranked.

By the eSIM Verdict research desk Last checked 18 July 2026 14 min read

Some links on eSIMVerdict are affiliate links. If you buy through them we may earn a commission at no extra cost to you. Commercial relationships never affect rankings or conclusions; our assessments are based on independent research.

Finding the best eSIM for multiple destinations comes down to one decision: a regional plan, a global plan, or several local eSIMs stitched together. Get it right and a three-week, four-country trip costs under $30 in data. Get it wrong and you pay a global-plan premium for countries you never visit, or discover at a land border that "Europe" did not include Montenegro.

Our research across five major providers found that Airalo's regional plans (Eurolink for Europe, Asialink for Asia) offer the strongest overall package for multi-country trips, while Nomad undercuts everyone on per-gigabyte price and Holiday.com makes repeat trips simplest with a single reusable eSIM. Holafly suits heavy data users who want unlimited plans, and MobiMatter is a marketplace where patient deal-hunters find regional bundles for around $1 per GB.

This guide compares current prices, exposes the coverage gaps hiding inside regional plans, and works through two real itineraries so you can see the actual cost of each approach.

Regional vs global plans: the real trade-offs

The per-GB premium is real

A regional plan buys data for one continent; a global plan buys the same data almost everywhere, and you pay handsomely for the flexibility. Airalo illustrates it neatly. Eurolink gives you 10GB across 42 European countries for $31. The Discover global plan charges $57 for 10GB, and Discover+ (which adds calls and texts through a US number) charges $79 for the same 10GB. That is roughly double to two-and-a-half times the per-GB cost for coverage you may not need.

Nomad shows the same pattern. Its Europe regional data starts from $0.70/GB at the 50GB tier, while its standard global plans start from around $5.15/GB. Only Nomad's long-validity Global-EX plans (10-50GB over 50-365 days, from $2.17/GB) narrow the gap, and they cover 54 destinations rather than 123.

The rule of thumb from our research: buy regional when your trip stays on one continent, and reserve global plans for genuinely intercontinental routes or year-long light use, such as Airalo's 20GB Discover plan valid for 365 days at $66.

Coverage gaps inside "Europe" plans

"Europe" is a marketing label, not a promise of the whole continent. The gaps cluster in the Western Balkans.

Holafly's Europe eSIM covers around 33 destinations including non-EU countries such as Switzerland, the UK, Norway and Iceland, but Albania, Serbia, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Montenegro, North Macedonia and Kosovo are absent. Holafly instead sells a separate Balkans eSIM covering five of those countries, at notably higher prices: $28.90 for 5 days of unlimited data versus $20.50 on the main Europe plan.

Airalo's Eurolink covers 42 countries and does reach much of the Balkans, but Kosovo does not appear on the published coverage list, and Airalo maintains a separate Balkans regional eSIM for a reason. Nomad's 35-destination Europe plan includes Switzerland, Serbia and Ukraine, with a dedicated 12-destination Balkans plan for deeper coverage.

Switzerland is the other classic trap. It sits outside the EU roaming area, so some budget "EU" plans skip it. All four fixed-price providers here include it in their main Europe products, but MobiMatter is a marketplace: one seller's "Europe 35GB" may include Switzerland while another's cheaper bundle quietly does not. Always open the country list before paying, whatever the provider. Our guide to eSIM reliability covers how to sanity-check coverage claims.

Validity windows for multi-week trips

Most unlimited plans top out at 30 days, which is fine for a holiday but awkward for a six-week wander. Fixed-data plans are more generous. Airalo sells 50GB/90-day ($100) and 100GB/180-day ($185) Eurolink packs, and its Asialink 100GB/180-day pack costs just $89. Nomad's Southeast Asia plans stretch to 45 and 60 days (20GB/45 days for $28, 30GB/60 days for $45), and its Global-EX plans run up to 365 days. Holafly's pay-as-you-go plans stop at 30 days for most regions (90 days on some), which is why it now pushes its monthly subscription for longer stays.

What happens when you cross a border

With a regional or global eSIM, nothing you need to manage. Your eSIM holds agreements with partner networks in every covered country, so when the train rolls from France into Switzerland your phone drops the French network and attaches to a Swiss partner automatically, drawing from the same data allowance. In practice the handover takes a few seconds to a few minutes; if it stalls, a quick toggle of aeroplane mode forces the reconnection. There are no new QR codes, no settings changes and no per-border fees, which is the entire argument for multi-country plans over collecting local SIMs. If you are weighing this against your home network's daily roaming charges, see eSIM vs roaming.

The five providers in detail

Airalo: the flexible all-rounder

Airalo covers 200+ locations with local, regional and global tiers, and its regional line-up is the most complete of any single provider. Eurolink (42 countries) runs from $5 for 1GB to $185 for 100GB/180 days, with a 20GB/30-day sweet spot at $48 and 10GB/30 days at $31. Asialink (18 countries) is cheaper still: 10GB/30 days for $25, 20GB/30 days for $39. Unlimited variants exist on both (Europe from $11.50 for 3 days to $72 for 30 days), structured as 3GB/day at full speed with reduced speed after.

The Discover global plans span 160+ countries, and Discover+ adds calls and texts via a US-based number, a rarity among travel eSIMs, which are otherwise data-only. Top-ups are available in the app (iOS 4.7, Android 4.6), so you never re-scan a QR code mid-trip. The main drawback is per-GB price: Nomad and MobiMatter regularly undercut it. Our full Airalo review digs deeper.

Pros: widest regional choice, long-validity packs, top-ups, optional calls/texts, polished apps. Cons: rarely the cheapest; no truly unlimited high-speed option; support is email and in-app chat rather than instant.

Nomad: the per-GB price leader

Nomad's regional catalogue is aggressive on price. Europe (35 destinations, including Switzerland and Serbia) sells 20GB/30 days for $27 and 50GB/30 days for $35, which is $0.70/GB. Southeast Asia-Oceania (8 countries, including Thailand, Vietnam, Singapore, Malaysia, Indonesia and the Philippines) offers 10GB/30 days for $22 and 20GB/45 days for $28. A 14-country APAC plan reaches $1.02/GB at the 50GB tier, and a dedicated 12-destination Balkans plan starts around $2.75/GB.

The catch is consistency. Some plans allow top-ups and some do not, "unlimited" means 2GB/day at speed then a 1Mbps crawl, and reinstall policies vary by plan, so read the small print before buying. Support is chat and email without the 24/7 guarantees of pricier rivals. For pure value it is the benchmark, as our best budget eSIM guide found.

Pros: lowest regional per-GB prices, long-validity SEA and Global-EX options, 5G on most plans. Cons: inconsistent top-up and reinstall rules, throttled "unlimited", support can be slow.

Holiday.com: one eSIM for every trip

Holiday.com, built by the ExpressVPN team (sold standalone, with no VPN included), takes a different approach to multi-destination travel: install one eSIM once, then top it up with plans for each new destination or trip. For serial travellers this removes the most annoying part of the eSIM routine, the install-delete-reinstall cycle, and keeps every trip in one account.

Coverage spans 150+ destinations with country plans from $3.50, plus worldwide multi-country plans (independent reviews cite roughly $4.50 for 1GB/5 days and about $35 for 10GB/30 days on worldwide coverage). Unlimited plans are unusually transparent: 3GB/day at high speed, then unlimited data at 10Mbps rather than an unusable crawl. Support is 24/7 live chat and email. The apps are newer than rivals', and Android setup may require manual APN entry, which is the main friction point our research surfaced.

Pros: one reusable eSIM across all trips, honest unlimited structure, 24/7 support, low entry prices. Cons: younger apps, Android APN fiddling, fewer destinations than Airalo or Holafly, per-GB cost on worldwide plans is mid-pack.

MobiMatter: the marketplace for deal-hunters

MobiMatter is not a single carrier but a marketplace listing plans from 30+ providers across 200+ destinations, and the deals can be startling. At the time of checking, a Sparks Europe promotion offered 10GB/30 days for $10.49 (about $1.05/GB), a NextLink Europe bundle 35GB for $26.99, and a 30GB yearly global plan for $34.99.

The flip side is variability. Coverage lists, top-up rights, hotspot rules, refund terms and support quality all depend on which underlying provider you buy from, and the buying experience is less polished than a single-brand app. Treat it like a flight-comparison site: excellent prices, but you must read each listing's terms yourself. See our Holafly vs MobiMatter comparison for how the marketplace model stacks up against a premium brand.

Pros: frequently the cheapest regional and global deals anywhere, huge choice, rewards programme. Cons: provider-dependent quality and refunds, inconsistent support, coverage varies listing by listing.

Holafly: unlimited data at a premium

Holafly sells simplicity: nearly every plan is unlimited, so there is no allowance arithmetic. The Europe eSIM costs $20.50 for 5 days, $50.50 for 15 and $73.90 for 30, covering around 33 destinations. The Global eSIM covers 142 destinations at a steep $46.90 for 7 days or $161.50 for 30. For long-term travellers there is a monthly subscription across 160+ destinations: 25GB for €45.95 or unlimited for €59.95 (about $64.90) per month, cancellable any time, with 15% off annual billing.

"Unlimited" carries conditions. Independent testing reports a soft cap of roughly 90GB of high-speed data per 30 days on the Europe plan, hotspot sharing is limited to about 1GB per day on trip plans, and pay-as-you-go plans cannot be topped up; you buy a new one if your dates change. The Balkans exclusion noted above also bites hardest here. Where Holafly shines is heavy use: streamers and hotspot-dependent workers who would burn through 20GB in a week come out ahead, as our Holafly vs Airalo head-to-head shows.

Pros: no data anxiety, 24/7 chat, monthly global subscription, wide destination count. Cons: expensive per usable GB, fair-use soft caps, hotspot limits, no top-ups on trip plans, Balkans sold separately.

When two local eSIMs beat one regional plan

Local plans are almost always cheaper per GB; Nomad sells Thai data from as little as $0.24/GB, and Airalo's cheapest local plans start at $4 for 1GB. For a two-country trip with a single border crossing, two local eSIMs can halve your data cost against a regional plan. The regional plan wins back its premium when you cross three or more borders, take day trips across frontiers, or simply do not want to install, activate and monitor multiple eSIMs. Below roughly $10 of total saving, our view is the convenience of one plan and automatic network switching is worth more than the difference.

Who should choose what

  • Multi-country Europe or Asia trips up to a month: Airalo Eurolink or Asialink if you value top-ups and app polish; Nomad if price per GB matters most.
  • Several trips a year: Holiday.com, because one installed eSIM re-tops for every journey.
  • Heavy streamers and hotspot workers: Holafly unlimited, with eyes open about the soft cap and the Balkans gap.
  • Long-term nomads: Holafly's monthly global subscription, or Airalo's 100GB/180-day regional packs at under $1/GB in Asia.
  • Budget maximisers who read small print: MobiMatter's marketplace deals.
  • Trips to the Western Balkans: Nomad's Europe or Balkans plans, or Airalo's Balkans eSIM; do not assume any "Europe" plan covers you.

The verdict

Airalo is the best eSIM for multiple destinations in 2026. Scored across our eight criteria (price, coverage, network quality, plan flexibility, transparency, ease of use, hotspot support, customer service), it leads with 65/80 thanks to the deepest regional catalogue, 180-day validity options, in-app top-ups and the only meaningful calls-and-texts option in the group. Nomad (61/80) is the value pick and wins outright for Southeast Asia. Holiday.com (61/80) ties it and takes the crown for frequent travellers through its one-eSIM re-top model and honest unlimited terms, held back only by app maturity. MobiMatter (54/80) delivers the lowest prices with the least consistency, and Holafly (53/80) remains the unlimited specialist you pay a clear premium for. Whichever you choose, open the country list first: the Balkans gap is the single most expensive surprise in multi-destination travel.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best eSIM for visiting multiple countries?

Airalo offers the strongest overall package, with regional plans such as Eurolink (42 European countries from $5) and Asialink (18 Asian countries from $4), plus global Discover plans covering 160+ countries. Nomad is the better choice if lowest cost per GB is your priority.

Do regional eSIMs switch networks automatically at borders?

Yes. When you enter a new covered country your phone attaches to a local partner network automatically, drawing from the same data allowance. It usually takes seconds; toggling aeroplane mode forces the switch if it is slow. No new QR code is needed.

Why doesn't my Europe eSIM work in the Balkans?

Many "Europe" plans exclude Western Balkan countries. Holafly's Europe plan omits Albania, Serbia, Bosnia, Montenegro, North Macedonia and Kosovo, selling a separate five-country Balkans eSIM instead, and Kosovo is absent from Airalo's Eurolink list. Check the published country list before every trip.

Is a global eSIM worth it over a regional plan?

Only for intercontinental routes or year-long light use. Airalo charges $31 for 10GB on its Europe regional plan but $57 for 10GB on its global Discover plan, a premium of over 80% for coverage a single-continent trip never uses.

Can I just buy separate local eSIMs for each country instead?

You can, and for one or two countries it is usually cheaper, with local plans from under $1/GB in parts of Asia. Beyond two or three borders, a single regional plan tends to win on convenience, automatic switching and the avoided hassle of managing several installs and expiry dates.
